HIST/EAST 213: Modern East Asia

What Did the United States Gain from the Opium Trade in China?

How New England merchants built fortunes, railroads, and elite institutions on drug money—and how we forgot.

William Daniell, "Canton Factories," c. 1805. Google Arts & Culture

The Basic Facts

American traders were the second largest opium dealers in China after the British.1 They operated primarily through Russell & Company, founded in Canton in 1824, which became "the largest American trading house in China" by the 1840s.2

Americans shipped Turkish opium (from Smyrna) to compete with British Indian opium. The competition "drove down the price of opium, leading to an increase in the availability of the drug for Chinese consumers."3

By the late 1820s, Russell & Company was delivering approximately 1,500 chests of opium annually to Canton, worth roughly $2 million.4

"The importation of opium that Western nations forced upon China was the most continued and systematic international crime of modern times."

— John King Fairbank, American China historian5

The Delano-Roosevelt Connection

Warren Delano II (1809–1898) went to Canton at age 24 and became Chief of Operations for Russell & Company. His "greatest achievement was the expansion of Russell & Company's trade in opium."6

Warren Delano claimed that opium smuggling was "a fair, honorable, and legitimate trade."7

After losing his fortune in the Panic of 1857, Delano returned to China in 1860 and rebuilt his wealth through the opium trade.8 During the Civil War, he shipped opium to the Medical Bureau of the U.S. War Department.9

The lineage: Warren Delano II → his daughter Sara Delano → married James Roosevelt → their son: Franklin Delano Roosevelt, 32nd President of the United States.10

From Opium to Railroads

John Murray Forbes "quickly made a fortune in Canton, working closely with the rich hong merchant Wu Bingjian (known as Howqua)." Howqua "and his family later appointed the company to manage their investments in the USA, including the financing of railroads."11

"When American merchants sold their Chinese cargoes on the American market, they converted Chinese goods into U.S. dollars, accumulating piles of capital... The capital from this trade fueled America's industrial revolution."

— Historian John Haddad, via WBUR12

Forbes became president of Michigan Central Railroad (1846–1855) and director of the Chicago, Burlington and Quincy Railroad.13

The Treaty of Wangxia (1844)

The United States gained "most favored nation" status through the Treaty of Wangxia, meaning Americans received all the treaty port privileges Britain had extracted through the Opium War—without fighting the war themselves.3

The irony: Article XXXIII of the treaty officially required the U.S. to prevent its citizens from trading opium. In practice, American merchants had already been smuggling opium for decades.14

How Americans Justified It

Warren Delano called it "a fair, honorable, and legitimate trade."7 Other justifications included:

  • "If we don't, the British will anyway"
  • Free trade ideology
  • Compartmentalization: opium was just "part of the business"
  • Distance: Boston society didn't see Canton

The Silence

The Forbes House Museum, in creating its Digital Opium Exhibit, describes the effort as "an effort by the Forbes House Museum trustees, staff and guides to confront the complicated history of harm, addiction, and wealth generated by the opium trade."15

Wu Bingjian (Howqua) was the hong merchant who partnered with American traders. His fortune was estimated at $26 million in 1834—making him one of the richest people in the world. He invested in American railroads through Forbes. Wikimedia Commons

The Human Cost

By 1900, an estimated 40 million Chinese—10% of the population—were addicted to opium. The drug drained silver from China's economy, destabilized families, and contributed to the dynasty's collapse.

American merchants like the Delanos and Forbes rationalized their role. The profits flowed back to Boston, New York, and Philadelphia—building the institutions we still use today.

"The importation of opium that Western nations forced upon China was the most continued and systematic international crime of modern times." — John King Fairbank

The Fortunes Made

Boston families that built American institutions with China trade profits

The Delanos

Russell & Company leadership

Warren Delano II was Chief of Operations for Russell & Co. in Macao, Canton, and Hong Kong. His "greatest achievement was the expansion of Russell & Company's trade in opium."6

Legacy: Grandfather of President Franklin Delano Roosevelt

The Forbes Brothers

Russell & Company partners

John Murray and Robert Bennet Forbes ran Russell & Co. from the 1830s. Robert managed opium sales from the Lintin storeship, earning over $30,000/year.11

Legacy: Chicago, Burlington & Quincy Railroad; Forbes family fortune

The Lows

Russell & Company partner

Abiel Abbot Low started as a clerk at Russell & Co. in 1833, became partner, then founded A.A. Low & Brother with the hong merchant Howqua.16

A.A. Low Building still stands at South Street Seaport in NYC—where China clipper ships once docked across the street.

Legacy: Low Memorial Library at Columbia University—built with $1 million from his son Seth Low17

The Russells

Company founders

Samuel Russell founded Russell & Company in Canton in 1824. His cousin William H. Russell co-founded Yale's Skull and Bones secret society.18

Legacy: Yale land; Skull & Bones; Russell Trust Association19

The Thirteen Factories at Canton — The only place where Westerners could legally trade with China. The American flag flies prominently among European nations. Russell & Company operated from this narrow strip of waterfront, where fortunes were made and opium deals arranged.

Timeline: American Opium Trade

1784

First American ship reaches Canton

The Empress of China arrives, beginning U.S.-China trade.

1824

Russell & Company founded

Samuel Russell establishes what becomes the largest American trading house in China.

1827

Russell & Co. becomes largest American opium dealer

Delivering ~1,500 chests annually to Canton.4

1839

Russell & Co. exits opium trade

One month before Commissioner Lin Zexu arrives to destroy British opium.

1842

Treaty of Nanjing ends First Opium War

Britain gains Hong Kong and treaty ports. U.S. watches.

1844

Treaty of Wangxia

U.S. gains "most favored nation" status—all British privileges, no war.3

1860

Warren Delano returns to China

After losing fortune in Panic of 1857, rebuilds wealth in Hong Kong opium trade.8

1891

Russell & Company closes

67 years of operation. Fortunes already invested in railroads, banks, institutions.
