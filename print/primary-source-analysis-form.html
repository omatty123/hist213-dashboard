2. Context — What world produced this source?

What was happening politically, socially, or culturally when this was written?

Who was the intended audience? Why does that matter?

What genre is this? What conventions shape it?

3. Argument — What is the author trying to do?

What is the author's main claim or purpose?

What assumptions does the author make? What goes unsaid?

Who or what is the author arguing against (even implicitly)?

4. Evidence — Key quotes

Copy 2-3 key quotes you'll use in your essay. Include page numbers.

5. Significance — So what?

What does this reveal about its time/place?

How does it connect to course themes?

What would we miss without this source?

Essay Structure (3 Pages)

Page 1: Introduction + Context

  • Hook: A striking quote or detail
  • Introduce the source (who, what, when)
  • Historical context (brief)
  • Thesis statement

Page 2: Analysis

  • Close reading of key passages
  • What the author argues + how
  • What's revealed vs. what's hidden
  • Use quotes as evidence

Page 3: Significance + Conclusion

  • Connect to broader course themes (modernity, encounters, reform, nationalism...)
  • What does this source help us understand that we couldn't see otherwise?
  • Conclusion: Restate thesis with greater nuance
