Why Send Half the Government Abroad?

In December 1871, just three years after the Meiji Restoration, Japan sent nearly half of its top leadership on a diplomatic mission around the world. The group included the three most powerful men in the new government‚ÄîIwakura Tomomi, ≈åkubo Toshimichi, and Kido Takayoshi‚Äîplus 48 officials and 59 students.

This was extraordinary‚Äîbut Japan wasn't alone. The Qing dynasty had sent the Burlingame Mission (1868) and would launch the Chinese Educational Mission (1872-1881) sending 120 students to America, overlapping exactly with Iwakura. Both East Asian powers recognized the need to learn from the West. The question is why their reform trajectories diverged.

Imagine the entire U.S. Cabinet, plus the Speaker of the House and key senators, leaving the country for two years. Who would run things? The answer: a "caretaker government" was left behind under Saig≈ç Takamori, with strict instructions not to make any major changes.

The mission had two official goals: renegotiate the unequal treaties imposed on Japan in the 1850s, and study Western institutions to determine what Japan should adopt.

"We must not follow blindly the footsteps of other nations, but must study their progress carefully and adapt only what suits our own circumstances."

‚Äî Kido Takayoshi, mission member

The Treaty Failure

The treaty renegotiation was a disaster. The Americans politely refused to revise the terms‚Äîand the Japanese delegation discovered they'd left Japan without the proper credentials to negotiate anyway. They had to send a ship back to get the documents, wasting months.

Every Western power gave the same answer: Japan could have treaty revision when it became "civilized"‚Äîmeaning when it had Western-style legal codes, courts, and institutions. This humiliation had a profound effect. The mission members realized that Japan would have to remake its entire society before it could be treated as an equal.

The Learning Mission

What the mission learned proved far more valuable than any treaty revision. Over 22 months, the delegates visited factories, shipyards, banks, schools, parliaments, and military installations. They took meticulous notes‚Äîlater compiled into a five-volume, 2,000-page report called the Bei≈ç Kairan Jikki (A True Account of the Tour of America and Europe).

The mission didn't simply catalog what they saw. They asked: What can Japan actually use? They were selective, pragmatic, and often critical. They admired British industry but found British society too class-bound. They respected American democracy but doubted it would work in Japan. They found the answer in an unexpected place: Germany.

The German Model

Newly unified Germany made the deepest impression. Here was a country that had achieved rapid industrialization and military power while maintaining authoritarian rule. The Prussian constitution gave the emperor (Kaiser) real power, unlike the British monarch. The education system produced loyal, disciplined citizens. The military was the best in Europe.

Most importantly, Germany had done all this recently. Britain's industrial revolution took a century; Germany modernized in decades. Japan could follow the same path.

The Five Female Students

Among the 59 students were five young women‚Äîthe first Japanese women sent abroad for education. The youngest, Tsuda Umeko, was just 6 years old.

Tsuda spent 11 years in America, returned to Japan, and became a pioneering educator. She founded what is now Tsuda University, one of Japan's leading women's colleges.

The mission recognized that Japan's modernization required educating women, not just men.

The Bei≈ç Kairan Jikki

The mission's official record, compiled by Kume Kunitake, was published in 1878 as 5 volumes totaling over 2,000 pages. A full English translation was published by The Japan Documents in 2002.

It documented everything: factory layouts, school curricula, parliamentary procedures, railroad gauges, military drills, banking systems, even Western dinner etiquette.

The report became a blueprint for Meiji reforms. Ministers referenced it for decades.

China's Parallel Missions

The Qing sent missions too. The Chinese Educational Mission (1872-1881) dispatched 120 students to America‚Äîat the exact same time as Iwakura.

These students thrived: entering Yale, MIT, learning English, playing baseball. But the program was cancelled in 1881 by conservative opposition and all students recalled.

The question isn't "Why didn't China try?" but "What structural factors made implementation harder?" Scale (400M vs 30M), the Taiping's 20-30M dead, provincial vs central power dynamics.

Discussion Questions

1. Both Japan and China sent study missions abroad in the 1870s. What structural differences might explain different outcomes? (Consider: scale, recent crises, elite composition, central vs provincial power.)

2. The mission chose the German model over British parliamentarism. What does this reveal about Meiji priorities?

3. Was the Iwakura Mission's approach "modernization" or "Westernization"? What's the difference?

The Mission's Legacy

The 1889 Constitution

It≈ç Hirobumi drafted Japan's constitution based on the German model‚Äîa strong emperor, limited parliament, and centralized power. It lasted until 1947.

Universal Education

By 1900, Japan had near-universal literacy. The American-inspired education system became a tool for creating loyal, productive citizens.

Industrial Takeoff

The mission identified key industries for state investment. By 1905, Japan was an industrial power capable of defeating Russia.
