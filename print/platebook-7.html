Penetrating Questions Key Points
1 Yi Ik: "The poor do not have enough land to stand an awl on" yet "the power of one man gags the mouths of the hundred" — what does this say about ideas vs. power? Reformers were yangban proposing to confiscate yangban land. Ideas alone don't change society — you need political power. None of these proposals was implemented.
2 How does Crown Prince Sado's tragedy illuminate the political system reformers tried to change? Sado's mental illness and murder by his father shows the suffocating pressure of Confucian court politics. Factionalism destroyed families. Same system that produced scholarship also produced cruelty.
3 Slavery declined 30%→10% without reform legislation. What does this tell us about how social change happens? Economic forces (commercialization, runaway slaves, hired labor) achieved what reformers couldn't legislate. Change sometimes comes from below, not elite proposals.
Connections
From Plate 6: Qianlong's letter to George III. Now Korean thinkers debate whether to learn from that same Qing — cultural pride vs. practical necessity.
Broader theme: Challenges "stagnant Asia" narrative. Koreans debated reform NOT because Westerners told them to, but because their own society was in crisis.
To Plate 8: Compare Tokugawa Japan — strict hierarchy to prevent change. Korea debated but couldn't implement. Both disrupted by Western pressure in 19th century.
Teaching Notes
• TWO READINGS: Balance SKT primary sources with Ebrey political/cultural context
• Yi Ik's "gag the mouths" quote is most memorable — use it!
• Crown Prince Sado story is dramatic — consider clip from "The Throne" (2015)
• "Those who are idle and yet eat their fill are the great parasites" resonates with students
• Ask: "What reforms do we know are needed today but seem politically impossible?"
