Penetrating Questions Key Points
1 "Our task today is to build up our own strength, not to destroy others." Why adopt non-violence against a violent colonizer? Strategic: couldn't win militarily. Moral: appeal to "awakening conscience of mankind." Religious: Buddhist & Christian signatories. Crushed but created lasting identity.
2 "Have we asked to become a colony of your country, a nation devoid of morality?" How does Korean nationalism judge Japan? By universal/international standards, not Asian traditions. Japan violated its own treaties (Shimonoseki). Korea claims moral high ground. Reverses "barbarian" rhetoric.
3 The Constitution proclaimed equality of "men and women, noble and common, rich and poor." How radical was this for 1919? Rejected yangban-commoner divide. Gender equality unprecedented (US women voted 1920). Nationalism as vehicle for social revolution.
Connections
From Plate 17: Japanese nationalism justified colonization. Now we see the response—moralistic, victim-centered, non-violent rather than Social Darwinist.
Broader theme: Colonialism creates nationalism, but content varies. Korean nationalism appeals to universal principles, not ethnic supremacy.
To Plate 19: Chinese nationalism (Lu Xun) turns inward—blaming China itself. Korean nationalism blamed Japan. Both from humiliation; different responses.
Teaching Notes
• READ ALOUD the Declaration's opening—it's eloquent and moving
• "Nation devoid of morality" is most quotable attack on Japan
• Ch'oe Namsŏn drafted but later collaborated—colonial complexity
• March 1 still a national holiday—this history lives today
• Both Koreas claim March First: North (armed struggle) vs South (non-violence)
