Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 Why did Hideyoshi believe conquering Korea and China was achievable, and what does this reveal about Japanese self-perception after unification? Discuss samurai confidence after civil war victories; limited knowledge of continental powers; need to redirect warrior energies; parallel to European overseas expansion
2 How did the Imjin War demonstrate both the strengths and limitations of the East Asian tributary system? Ming intervention showed system worked defensively; but also revealed Ming overextension; Korea as 'lips and teeth' to China; costs to all parties
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 3: Plates 1-3 established the cultural, religious, and conceptual frameworks of East Asia, including ideas of modernity. The Imjin War shows these civilizations in violent collision—testing Confucian governance, Buddhist institutions, and regional hierarchies.
Context: The Imjin War prefigures later Japanese imperialism in Korea (1876-1945); demonstrates how domestic political dynamics drive foreign policy; raises questions about how wars are remembered differently by victors, losers, and victims.
To Plate 5: Plate 5 (Rise of Qing) directly follows: the Ming exhaustion from defending Korea contributed to their vulnerability to Manchu conquest. The war's costs accelerated Ming decline.
Notes
• Students may ask about parallels to Japan's 20th-century colonization of Korea—worth discussing but keep focus on 16th century context
• The reading presents Japanese perspectives; balance with discussion of Korean experience and memory
• Common misconception: Japan was technologically superior—actually, Korean turtle ships and Chinese numbers offset Japanese infantry advantages
• Visual aids: Map showing invasion routes and Yi Sun-sin's naval battles; images of turtle ships
• Engaging story: Yi Sun-sin's final battle at Noryang, where he was killed in the moment of victory, is deeply moving
