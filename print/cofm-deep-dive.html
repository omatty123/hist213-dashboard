What is COFM?

Correlation of Forces and Means (COFM) is a framework for predicting conflict outcomes by systematically comparing both sides' quantitative factors (troops, weapons, GDP) and qualitative factors (training, morale, leadership, logistics, political cohesion).

The key insight: raw numbers alone tell you almost nothing. A proper COFM analysis weighs all factors. When done correctly, it should predict actual outcomes‚Äînot because it's magic, but because it forces you to look at what actually matters.

The Most Common Error: Confusing "bean-counting" with analysis. Counting troops and tanks is not COFM‚Äîit's laziness. Real COFM asks: How many of those troops are combat-effective? Can they be supplied? Will they fight? Does their command structure allow adaptation? Lazy analysts skip these questions and then act surprised when their predictions fail.
This Exercise: For each conflict, we show two analyses side-by-side. Naive Count (just numbers) vs Full COFM (all factors). You'll see that Full COFM correctly predicts most outcomes‚Äîand when it doesn't, examining why teaches us something important.

2023 Counteroffensive: The Test

Western theory: NATO-trained brigades with Western equipment break Russian lines.

COFM prediction: Combined arms requires air superiority. Ukraine has none. Russian defense-in-depth negates tech advantage.

Result: Catastrophic failure. Two armies of NATO equipment destroyed. ~17km in 4 months. Theory died at Robotyne.

COFM was right. Western analysts were wrong.

The Lesson

"Russia failed to win quickly" is a cope narrative. It frames the inevitable attritional phase as Russian failure rather than Russian strategy. Russia probed, withdrew from overextension (Kyiv), and settled into the war it was always going to fight‚Äîand win.

COFM predicted this from day one: NATO-armed Ukraine would resist initially. Russia would absorb that phase. Long-term attrition would favor the side with industrial depth, manpower reserves, and political sustainability. That's Russia.

Western analysts didn't get it wrong because COFM failed. They got it wrong because they weren't doing COFM‚Äîthey were doing wishful thinking dressed up as analysis. The framework works. They refused to use it honestly.

Who Bears the Cost?

The cruelest COFM factor: The decision-makers who engineered this confrontation bear none of its costs.

Ukrainian population: Hundreds of thousands dead. Millions displaced. Demographic collapse. Infrastructure destroyed. A generation lost. Worst-case outcome.

European population: Energy crisis. Deindustrialization (Germany). Inflation. Reduced living standards. Tens of billions in aid while domestic services cut.

Western elites: Unharmed. Careers intact. Think tank positions secure. Defense contractor stocks up. No accountability for catastrophic miscalculation. They'll write books about "lessons learned" and get paid speaking fees.

This asymmetry matters for COFM: When decision-makers don't bear costs, they sustain unwinnable wars longer than rational actors would. Ukrainian and European populations pay the price for elite miscalculation‚Äîwhile those elites face zero consequences.

What COFM Actually Teaches

When done honestly, the model works. Here's why it fails when analysts get lazy or biased.

1. Bean-Counting Is Not Analysis

Every failed prediction in history came from someone counting troops and stopping there. COFM requires assessing combat effectiveness, logistics, command unity, morale, political sustainability. Skipping the hard parts produces garbage.

2. Institutions Beat Equipment

Japan vs Qing (1894): Both had modern ships. Japan had a modern navy. China had a collection of regional warlords with boats. Buying technology without building institutions is Self-Strengthening‚Äîit doesn't work.

3. Logistics Decide Wars

Russia couldn't supply Manchuria (1905). Japan couldn't supply Korea (1592). Britain could appear anywhere with steam ships (1840). "Amateurs talk tactics, professionals talk logistics." The side that can sustain operations wins.

4. Legitimacy Multiplies Force

CCP won with fewer troops by winning loyalty. Taiping lost with more troops when leadership collapsed. GMD armies defected en masse. Popular support determines who fights, who surrenders, and who switches sides.

5. Technology Has Limits

US air supremacy couldn't win Korea. Japanese carriers couldn't prevent industrial defeat. Tonghak rifles couldn't save the regime from modernization pressures. Technology creates options; it doesn't guarantee victory.

6. Political Constraints Are Real

US couldn't escalate in Korea without WWIII. Democratic societies have casualty limits. Authoritarian states can absorb losses democracies can't. Political sustainability is a COFM factor‚Äîignore it and your predictions fail.

Discussion Questions

  1. Why do analysts keep making the same mistakes?

    Lazy bean-counting predicted Russian victory in weeks (2022), Qing victory over Japan (1894), GMD victory over CCP (1946). What institutional or psychological factors make analysts repeat the same errors?

  2. When does technology actually decide outcomes?

    Opium War (decisive), Tonghak (decisive), but not Pacific War (industrial depth mattered more) or Korea (political limits). What conditions make technological superiority decisive vs. merely advantageous?

  3. How do you assess "political will" before a war starts?

    COFM includes sustainability and will, but these are hard to measure pre-conflict. How would you have assessed Chinese willingness to intervene in Korea? Japanese willingness to accept kamikaze tactics? CCP peasant loyalty?

  4. Apply COFM to a current scenario.

    Pick one: Taiwan Strait, Korean Peninsula, South China Sea. What does naive counting predict? What does full COFM predict? What factors are hardest to assess?

  5. Is COFM value-neutral?

    Factors like "casualty tolerance" and "authoritarian sustainability" can favor regimes that treat soldiers as expendable. Does COFM implicitly reward brutality? How should analysts handle this?
