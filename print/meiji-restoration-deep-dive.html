HIST/EAST 213: Modern East Asia

The Domains That Toppled the Shogun

How four "outside" domains—excluded from power for 250 years—formed an unlikely alliance, overthrew the Tokugawa shogunate, and launched Japan into modernity.

Battle of Toba-Fushimi, 1868. Wikimedia Commons

Geography of Revolution

The four domains that led the Meiji Restoration were all tozama ("outside") lords—clans that had opposed Tokugawa Ieyasu at Sekigahara in 1600 and were excluded from the shogunate's inner circle for over two centuries.

The Tozama Problem

When Tokugawa Ieyasu unified Japan after the Battle of Sekigahara (1600), he divided the daimyō into categories. The fudai (hereditary vassals) who had supported him received domains near Edo and positions in the shogunate. The tozama (outside lords) who had opposed him—or joined too late—were pushed to the periphery.

For 250 years, tozama domains like Satsuma and Chōshū were excluded from the shogunate's decision-making. They could not hold positions in the central government. But this exclusion had an unintended consequence: they had no stake in preserving the system.

"Revere the Emperor, Expel the Barbarians"

尊王攘夷 (sonnō jōi) — the rallying cry that united anti-shogunate forces

From Rivals to Allies

Satsuma and Chōshū had been bitter rivals. In 1863, when Chōshū radicals attempted a coup in Kyoto, Satsuma helped the shogunate drive them out. The two domains seemed destined for war.

But the foreign crisis changed everything. After British warships bombarded Kagoshima (1863) and a combined Western fleet destroyed Chōshū's coastal defenses (1864), both domains realized that fighting foreigners was futile—and that the shogunate was too weak to protect Japan.

In 1866, the unthinkable happened: Sakamoto Ryōma, a Tosa samurai acting as intermediary, brokered the secret Satchō Alliance (薩長同盟). Former enemies agreed to work together against the shogunate.

The Restoration

On January 3, 1868, Satsuma and Chōshū forces seized the imperial palace in Kyoto and announced the "restoration" of imperial rule. The teenage Emperor Meiji—just 15 years old—became the symbolic center of a new government.

The shogun, Tokugawa Yoshinobu, had already returned his political authority to the emperor weeks earlier, hoping to remain influential in a new coalition government. But the Satchō leaders maneuvered to exclude him entirely, provoking the Boshin War (1868–1869).

Within 18 months, the last Tokugawa loyalists surrendered. The 265-year shogunate was over.

Why Did They Win?

Superior weapons: Both Satsuma and Chōshū had been secretly purchasing modern rifles and artillery from Western merchants—including through Thomas Glover in Nagasaki.

Military reforms: Chōshū's Kiheitai (奇兵隊) mixed samurai and commoners, breaking the class monopoly on warfare. Satsuma trained a modern navy.

Imperial legitimacy: By claiming to act in the emperor's name, the rebels transformed from traitors into loyalists overnight.

The Sekigahara Grudge

At Sekigahara (1600), the Shimazu of Satsuma and Mōri of Chōshū fought against Tokugawa Ieyasu. They lost—and spent 250 years nursing the grudge.

Mōri clan tradition held that every New Year's morning, a retainer would ask: "Has the time come to overthrow the Tokugawa?" The answer was always: "Not yet."

In 1868, the answer finally changed.

Key Figures

Saigō Takamori (Satsuma) — Military leader of the restoration forces. Later led the Satsuma Rebellion (1877) against the government he helped create.

Ōkubo Toshimichi (Satsuma) — Political strategist. Dominated early Meiji government until his assassination in 1878.

Kido Takayoshi (Chōshū) — Helped draft the Charter Oath. Architect of the abolition of domains.

Sakamoto Ryōma (Tosa) — Brokered the Satchō Alliance. Assassinated in 1867, just before the restoration.

The Four Great Domains

薩長土肥 (Sacchōdohi) — The alliance that made modern Japan

Satsuma

薩摩藩
Naval power · Early modernizers

Located in southern Kyushu, Satsuma was Japan's second-largest domain. The Shimazu clan controlled the Ryukyu Kingdom and maintained secret trade with China, accumulating wealth outside shogunal oversight.

After the British bombardment of Kagoshima (1863), Satsuma pivoted from anti-foreign resistance to aggressive modernization, purchasing Western warships and training a modern navy.

Key leaders: Saigō Takamori, Ōkubo Toshimichi

Chōshū

長州藩
Military reformers · Radical ideologues

The Mōri clan's domain at the western tip of Honshu controlled the strategic Shimonoseki Strait. Chōshū became the center of sonnō jōi ("revere the emperor, expel the barbarians") radicalism.

After Western powers destroyed their coastal batteries (1864), Chōshū reformed its military, creating mixed-class militia units (Kiheitai) armed with modern weapons.

Key leaders: Kido Takayoshi, Itō Hirobumi, Yamagata Aritomo

Tosa

土佐藩
Constitutional moderates · Brokers

On the Pacific coast of Shikoku, Tosa produced key intermediaries who bridged the Satsuma-Chōshū divide. Tosa leaders favored a constitutional settlement that would include the shogun.

When the hardliners prevailed, Tosa still contributed forces to the imperial army—but later became a center of opposition to Satchō dominance and the movement for elected assemblies.

Key leaders: Sakamoto Ryōma, Itagaki Taisuke

Hizen

肥前藩
Technology · Industry

Based in Saga in northern Kyushu, Hizen was Japan's leader in Western technology. The Nabeshima clan built Japan's first reverberatory furnace, cannon foundry, and experimental steam engine.

Though less prominent in the military campaigns, Hizen contributed crucial technical expertise to the new government and dominated early industrialization efforts.

Key leaders: Ōkuma Shigenobu, Etō Shinpei

Timeline: Fall of the Shogunate

1853

Perry arrives

American "Black Ships" force Japan to open ports, exposing shogunal weakness.

1863

Bombardment of Kagoshima

British warships destroy much of Satsuma's capital after the Namamugi Incident. Satsuma pivots to modernization.

1864

Shimonoseki Campaign

Combined Western fleet destroys Chōshū's coastal defenses. Both major anti-foreign domains now recognize the need to modernize.

1866

Satchō Alliance

Sakamoto Ryōma brokers secret agreement between former rivals Satsuma and Chōshū. The shogunate's days are numbered.

1867

Shogun returns authority

Tokugawa Yoshinobu surrenders political power to Emperor Meiji, hoping to remain influential. He is outmaneuvered.

1868

Meiji Restoration

January 3: Imperial forces seize power in Kyoto. The Boshin War begins; Tokugawa loyalists are defeated within 18 months.

1871

Abolition of domains

The new government abolishes all 270+ domains, replacing them with centrally-controlled prefectures. The samurai era ends.
