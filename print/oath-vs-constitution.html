Charter Oath vs Meiji Constitution

Promise vs. Reality — What Meiji Japan actually delivered

1868

Charter Oath

五箇条の御誓文 (Gokajō no Goseimon)

What it promised
  • "Deliberative assemblies" established
  • "All matters decided by public discussion"
  • "All classes united" in national effort
  • "Evil customs of the past" abandoned
  • "Knowledge sought throughout the world"
What it lacked
  • Who gets to participate in assemblies?
  • What counts as "public"?
  • Any enforcement mechanism
  • Timeline for implementation
  • Definition of rights
"Deliberately vague — could mean democracy or oligarchy depending on who's reading." — The strategic ambiguity was the point
1889

Meiji Constitution

大日本帝国憲法 (Dai-Nippon Teikoku Kenpō)

What it delivered
  • Diet (parliament) with two houses
  • Limited voting rights (1% of population)
  • Some civil liberties "within the law"
  • Cabinet and ministries
  • Written legal framework
What it kept from the people
  • Emperor remained sovereign (not the people)
  • Emperor could dissolve Diet at will
  • Military answered to emperor, not Diet
  • No universal suffrage (until 1925)
  • Rights could be suspended "by law"
"The Emperor is sacred and inviolable." — Article 3, Meiji Constitution

TL;DR for discussion

Charter Oath: Revolutionary-sounding rhetoric, zero specifics. Compare to "all men are created equal" — founding documents often promise more than they deliver.

Constitution: Created a parliament but kept real power with the emperor and oligarchs. The same men who wrote the Charter Oath decided what it "really meant" 21 years later — and surprise, it meant they stayed in charge.
