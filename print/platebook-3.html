Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 If China was wealthier than Europe until 1800, why do we think of 'modernity' as a European invention? Power shapes historical narratives. European colonialism coincided with the writing of 'universal' history. Consider: Who wrote the textbooks? Whose archives survived? Who controlled universities?
2 Is 'modernization' the same as 'Westernization'? This question will recur throughout the course. Japan's Meiji era, China's self-strengthening movement, Korea's reforms—were these adoptions of Western modernity or something else? Can you be 'modern' without being 'Western'?
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 2: Plates 1-2 established East Asia's geographic and cultural foundations. Now we question the framework through which we interpret its history.
Context: This framework informs the entire course. When we discuss 'unequal treaties,' 'opening' of Japan, 'modernization' efforts—who defines these terms and from whose perspective?
To Plate 4: Plate 4 on the Imjin War begins our chronological journey, and we should keep asking: How do our assumptions about 'modernity' shape how we interpret these events?
Notes
• Start with Asia for Educators videos—they're accessible and set up Frank's denser argument well.
• Frank is a SKIM reading. Students should find: (1) thesis, (2) main evidence. Don't expect them to read all 51 pages closely.
• Some students will resist Frank's argument as 'politically correct revisionism.' Welcome the debate—that's the point!
• Connect to current events: articles about 'China's rise' often frame it as unprecedented. Is it?
• Clarify: Frank isn't saying Europe accomplished nothing, but that its 'rise' was contingent on global conditions, not internal 'genius.'
