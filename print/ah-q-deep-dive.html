Week 7 · Plate 19

The True Story of Ah Q

Lu Xun's Satire of Chinese National Character
Lu Xun published this novella in serialized form in the Beijing Morning Post supplement from December 1921 to February 1922. Through the pathetic figure of Ah Q, a rural day-laborer who transforms every defeat into a "spiritual victory," Lu Xun diagnosed what he saw as China's deepest sickness: not foreign aggression, but the national habit of self-deception. Where Korean nationalism (Plate 18) blamed Japan, Lu Xun blamed China itself.

Chapter-by-Chapter Analysis
The Problem of Writing
Chapter 1 · pp. 1-6 · Introduction
The Impossible Biography
Lu Xun frames the novella as a mock-scholarly exercise in writing about nobody
The narrator spends six pages explaining why he can't write this biography. Ah Q has no verified surname, no proper given name, no hometown anyone can confirm, no ancestry worth recording. Lu Xun is mocking the entire tradition of Chinese biographical writing, which existed to honor great men. Ah Q is the anti-great-man: a nobody whose story exposes everything the tradition hid.
"For several years now I have been meaning to write the true story of Ah Q. But while wanting to write I was in some trepidation, too, which goes to show that I am not one of those who achieve glory by writing."
p. 1
The Confucian injunction "If the name is not correct, the words will not ring true" becomes a running joke. Lu Xun can't even name his subject. This isn't just comedy; it's a statement about who Chinese culture has deemed worth remembering.
The Spiritual Victory Method
Chapters 2-3 · pp. 7-19 · Ah Q's Victories
Beaten by My Son
The core mechanism of self-deception, demonstrated in escalating episodes
Ah Q gets mocked for his ringworm scars, beaten by idlers, slapped by the Chao family's bailiff, and robbed of his gambling winnings. Each time, he transforms the humiliation into a psychological triumph. Beaten and head-bumped against the wall? "It is as if I were beaten by my son." Called the "foremost self-belittler"? Subtract the "self-belittler" and what remains is "foremost." This isn't resilience. It's the systematic evasion of reality.
"Ah Q would stand there for a second, thinking to himself, 'It is as if I were beaten by my son. What is the world coming to nowadays. . . .' Thereupon he too would walk away, satisfied at having won."
p. 10
The most devastating moment comes after the gambling scene. Robbed of his silver, even the "beaten by my son" formula fails. So Ah Q slaps his own face until the pain makes it feel like someone else was hit. He falls asleep "satisfied that he had gained the victory."
"Raising his right hand he slapped his own face hard twice, so that it tingled with pain. After this slapping his heart felt lighter, for it seemed as if the one who had given the slap was himself, the one slapped some other self, and soon it was just as if he had beaten someone else."
p. 13
Social Hierarchy & Humiliation
Chapter 4 · pp. 20-28 · The Tragedy of Love
Ah Q and the Amah
Sexual desire meets class boundary, with disastrous results
Ah Q kneels before Amah Wu, the Chao family's maidservant, and begs her to "sleep with" him. The scene is simultaneously pathetic and horrifying. Ah Q has no concept of courtship, consent, or even basic human interaction. He understands only hierarchy: you take what you can from those below you, and endure what comes from those above. His "proposal" gets him beaten, fined, and permanently expelled from the Chao household's economy. This chapter shows the damage hierarchy inflicts on people at the bottom: they can't even conceive of human relationships outside the master-servant framework.
Chapters 5-6 · pp. 29-43 · Livelihood & Decline
The Cycle of Abuse
Ah Q bullies those weaker, submits to those stronger, and calls it natural order
Expelled from Weichuang's economy after the Amah Wu incident, Ah Q becomes a petty thief. He steals from a convent, bullying an old nun. He briefly returns to Weichuang with stolen goods, enjoys a burst of respect, then loses it again when the villagers realize his wares are stolen. Lu Xun's point is structural: in this society, people only respect those who can hurt them. Ah Q is a bully to the weak and a coward to the strong. So is everyone else.
Revolution as Farce
Chapters 7-8 · pp. 44-59 · The Revolution / Barred from the Revolution
"Rebellion! Rebellion!"
The 1911 Revolution arrives in Weichuang. Nothing changes.
News of revolution reaches the village. The gentry panic. Ah Q, who has "always detested and kept away" from revolutionaries, suddenly decides revolution is "not a bad thing." His revolutionary fantasy is pure ego: he imagines men in white armor calling "Ah Q! Come with us!" and himself deciding which villagers to spare, which women to claim, which furniture to steal. The revolution, in Ah Q's mind, is just a chance to become the bully instead of the bullied.
"'Revolution is not a bad thing,' thought Ah Q. 'Finish off the whole lot of them . . . curse them! . . . I would like to go over to the revolutionaries myself.'"
p. 46
But the village elite simply cut their queues, call themselves revolutionaries, and continue running everything. The "Imitation Foreign Devil" bars Ah Q from joining. The revolution is a costume change, not a transformation. Lu Xun's satire of the 1911 Revolution is unsparing: it changed the decorations, not the structure. The old hierarchy survived intact.
"'There is a revolution . . . don't you know?' said Ah Q vaguely. 'Revolution, revolution . . . there has already been one,' said the old nun, her eyes red from crying. 'What do you think will become of us with all your revolutions?'"
p. 49
The Grand Finale
Chapter 9 · pp. 60-67 · The Grand Finale
The Circle That Couldn't Close
Ah Q is arrested, tried, and executed for a crime he didn't commit
The Chao family is robbed. The new revolutionary government needs a culprit. Ah Q is arrested, dragged before a court he doesn't understand, and asked to sign a confession. He can't write. They tell him to draw a circle instead. He tries desperately to make it round, fails, and produces "a shape like a melon seed." This is one of the most famous scenes in modern Chinese literature: the illiterate man trying and failing to make a perfect circle as his death warrant.
"'I — I — can't write,' said Ah Q, shamefaced, nervously holding the brush."
p. 62
On the way to execution, Ah Q still doesn't fully grasp what's happening. He manages one last attempt at a spiritual victory: "In twenty years I shall be another." The crowd roars approval. The spectators are disappointed because a shooting isn't as entertaining as a beheading, and Ah Q didn't even sing an opera excerpt on the way. Everyone has missed the point of everything.
"Most people were dissatisfied, because a shooting was not such a fine spectacle as a decapitation; and what a ridiculous culprit he had been too, to pass through so many streets without singing a single line from an opera. They had followed him for nothing."
p. 67
About the Author
Lu Xun
鲁迅 (birth name: Zhou Shuren 周树人)
1881-1936
Writer & Cultural Critic
Born into a declining scholar-gentry family in Shaoxing, Zhejiang (Britannica). His grandfather's imprisonment for examination fraud (1893) (Columbia AFE) and his father's death (1896) exposed him early to social humiliation and the failures of traditional medicine. These experiences shaped his lifelong project: diagnosing China's cultural sickness.
Legacy: Mao called him "the commander of China's cultural revolution" (Columbia AFE; from Mao's 1940 essay "On New Democracy"). The "prison or silence" remark is widely cited but unverified: attributed to a 1957 conversation, no primary source has been located. Treat as apocryphal.
The Lantern Slide Incident (1906)
幻灯片事件
Sendai Medical Academy, Japan
Turning Point
While studying medicine in Sendai, Lu Xun saw a wartime lantern slide showing Chinese bystanders watching apathetically as a compatriot was executed by the Japanese during the Russo-Japanese War (Britannica; New World Encyclopedia). He concluded that medicine could heal bodies, but China's sickness was spiritual. He abandoned medicine for literature.
His logic: "The people of a weak and backward country, however healthy and strong they might be, could only serve to be made examples of or as witnesses of such futile spectacles." (Lu Xun, preface to Call to Arms, 1922.) Physical health without spiritual awakening was meaningless.
Key Concepts
Spiritual Victory
精神胜利法 (jingshen shengli fa)
Ah Q's method of reinterpreting every defeat as a triumph. Beaten? "Beaten by my son." Robbed? Slap yourself until you feel like you slapped someone else. Lu Xun's metaphor for how China responded to the century of humiliation: claiming moral superiority while losing every actual contest.
The Iron House
铁屋子 (tie wuzi)
From Lu Xun's preface to Call to Arms: an airtight room full of sleeping people about to suffocate. Is it crueler to wake them to die in agony, or let them die in sleep? Lu Xun chose to wake them. Literature was his alarm.
National Character
国民性 (guominxing)
The idea that a nation has essential psychological traits that can be diagnosed and treated. Lu Xun believed China's national character was sick: self-deceiving, hierarchical, cowardly. Controversial because it can slide into essentialism, but central to May Fourth thinking.
Reading Guide
What to Watch For
The narrator's ironic distance. He never tells you directly what to think. Pay attention to what he describes without commentary.
Every time Ah Q "wins." Track how the spiritual victory method escalates from mild self-deception to slapping his own face.
The social hierarchy of Weichuang. Who bullies whom? The Chao family bullies Ah Q. Ah Q bullies the nun and Whiskers Wang. Everyone bullies those below them.
The revolution chapters (7-8). What actually changes in Weichuang after the revolution? Who benefits? Who gets punished?
The circle scene (Ch. 9, p. 62). Ah Q trying to draw a perfect circle as his confession. Why does Lu Xun make this the climax?
The crowd at the execution (p. 67). They're disappointed because it wasn't entertaining enough. What is Lu Xun saying about spectatorship?
Compare with Plates 17-18

Japanese nationalism (Plate 17): Expansionist, confident, Social Darwinist. Japan's problem was external: how to dominate. Tokutomi Soho celebrated power.

Korean nationalism (Plate 18): Defensive, moral, victimhood-based. Korea's problem was external: Japanese aggression. The Declaration of Independence appealed to universal justice.

Chinese nationalism (Plate 19): Self-critical, satirical, directed inward. China's problem was internal: a culture that taught people to deceive themselves rather than face reality. Lu Xun attacked Chinese tradition to save the Chinese nation.

Lu Xun's Major Works
"Diary of a Madman" (1918). First major modern Chinese story. A narrator discovers Chinese history is a record of "eating people."
Call to Arms (1923). Story collection including "Ah Q," "Kong Yiji," "Medicine." The foundational texts of modern Chinese literature.
Wandering (1926). Darker stories including "The New Year's Sacrifice," about a widow destroyed by patriarchal norms.
Wild Grass (1927). Prose poems. Lu Xun at his most personal: anguish, despair, the paradox of hoping without grounds for hope.
Essays (zawen) (1927-1936). Hundreds of short polemical pieces. Sharp, funny, merciless. His main output in the Shanghai years.

Why Ah Q Still Matters

"The Ah Q spirit" (阿Q精神) entered Chinese as a permanent term for self-deception in the face of defeat. Lu Xun's novella is still taught in Chinese schools, and the phrase is still used in Chinese media and everyday conversation when someone rationalizes a loss. The concept has outlived the specific historical moment that produced it.

Lu Xun's critique cuts deeper than politics. The 1911 Revolution failed, in his telling, not because of military weakness or bad leadership, but because the people it was supposed to liberate had no concept of liberation. Ah Q wants revolution so he can become the bully. The village elite want revolution so they can keep their positions under a new name. Nobody wants actual transformation. The revolution changes costumes, not structures.

This self-critical nationalism stands in radical contrast to the defensive nationalisms of Korea and Japan. Korean nationalists (Plate 18) said: "We are victims of injustice." Japanese nationalists (Plate 17) said: "We are destined to lead." Lu Xun said: "We are sick, and we did it to ourselves." All three diagnoses were responses to the same crisis of Western imperialism in East Asia. But they pointed to completely different prescriptions, and those prescriptions would shape the twentieth century.
