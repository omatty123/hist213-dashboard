A note on Mao-era data. Official Chinese statistics for 1958–1960 are unreliable. During the Great Leap Forward, local cadres massively over-reported grain yields and industrial output to meet impossible quotas. The NBS figure of 21.3% for 1958 almost certainly overstates real growth. The famine's true economic impact (1959–1961) was also likely deeper than official figures suggest. World Bank data begins in 1961. NBS data from 1952 was retroactively reconstructed in 1997.

Recovery & First Five-Year Plan

1953–1957
9.4% avg

Soviet-aided industrialization. Heavy industry prioritized. Land reform completed. Modest but real growth built on post-civil-war recovery.

Great Leap Forward

1958–1962
-0.4% avg*

Backyard steel, collectivized farming, falsified statistics. The Great Chinese Famine killed 15–55 million. Official 1958 numbers are inflated. The 1961 crash of -27.3% is the single worst year in PRC history.

Post-Leap Recovery

1963–1965
15.1% avg

Liu Shaoqi and Deng Xiaoping managed the recovery. Pragmatic policies restored agriculture. High growth rates partly reflect rebound from an extremely low base.

Cultural Revolution

1966–1976
5.3% avg

Political chaos, but industry kept running. Worst years: 1967 (-5.7%) and 1968 (-4.1%) when Red Guard violence peaked. Mao's death in 1976 coincided with -1.6% contraction.

Reform & Opening

1978–1991
9.3% avg

Deng's SEZs, household responsibility system, TVEs. Growth surged to 15.2% in 1984. Tiananmen (1989) and austerity brought it down to 3.9% in 1990, the lowest of the reform era.

Boom Years

1992–2007
10.5% avg

Deng's Southern Tour (1992) re-ignited reform. WTO accession (2001) integrated China into global supply chains. GDP growth hit 14.2% in 2007. China became the world's factory.

New Normal

2008–2019
7.7% avg

Global financial crisis, massive stimulus (4 trillion yuan). Xi Jinping's "new normal" accepted slower growth. Shift from investment to consumption. Trade war with US (2018–).

COVID & After

2020–2025
4.9% avg

Zero-COVID cratered 2020 (2.3%) and 2022 (3.1%). Property crisis (Evergrande). Youth unemployment. 2024–25 target of "around 5%" met both years, but each figure invites scrutiny.

The Teaching Question

Is the CPC's legitimacy built on growth? What happens if 5% becomes the ceiling? The Party survived Mao-era chaos (avg 4.8%) but today's citizens have different expectations.
