"The use of Chinese characters created a literary lingua franca that enabled educated elites across East Asia to communicate through writing even when they could not understand each other's spoken languages." — Schoppa, Basic Identities
Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 What makes 'East Asia' a coherent cultural region rather than just a geographic label? Focus on shared writing system, Confucian values, Buddhist practices, and governmental models. But also note variations—each country adapted borrowings. Is 'East Asia' a useful category or does it obscure important differences?
2 How did the Chinese writing system both unite and divide East Asian societies? United: created shared literary culture, enabled diplomatic communication. Divided: created elite/commoner gap (literacy required years of study), eventually led each country to develop phonetic systems for common people.
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 1: Plate 1 questioned what 'East Asia' means and challenged Eurocentric assumptions. Now we examine what actually defines the region culturally.
Context: The shared cultural foundations established here will help explain both East Asian responses to Western imperialism and the different paths each country took toward modernity.
To Plate 3: Plate 3 on 'What is Modernity?' will ask how these traditional foundations were challenged and transformed by encounters with the West.
Notes
• Bring examples of Chinese characters and their Japanese/Korean variants to show adaptation.
• Consider showing a map of Buddhism's spread from India through Central Asia to China and then to Korea/Japan.
• Students often assume Confucianism is a 'religion'—clarify it's more of a social philosophy (though it has religious dimensions).
• Discussion prompt: Are there parallels to how American culture spreads globally today? How do other countries adapt it?
• Be careful not to essentialize—not all East Asians are Confucian, and these traditions continue to evolve.
