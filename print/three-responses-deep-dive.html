Three Responses to Western Imperialism

Meiji Restoration · Taiping Rebellion · Tonghak Rebellion — Same crisis, radically different answers. One succeeded. Two were crushed. Why?

HIST / EAST 213 — Deep Dive
The question that haunted East Asia after 1860

“The Westerners cultivated high ethics, and there is nothing they cannot do. There is no one who can stand before their attacking military power, and even China is being destroyed.” — Ch’oe Cheu, On Learning Truth (1861)

By 1860, the old order was finished. Anglo-French troops had burned the Summer Palace in Beijing. The Qing were signing away sovereignty in unequal treaties. Perry’s Black Ships had already forced open Japan. Korea watched from the sidelines, terrified it was next. Every society in East Asia faced the same existential question: What do we do about the West?

Japan Surrender of Edo Castle, 1868 — Yuki Somei (Meiji Memorial Gallery)

Meiji Restoration

1868 — Japan

Elite samurai overthrew the shogunate and adopted Western methods to beat the West at its own game. “Rich country, strong army.”

China Regaining Jinling (Nanjing) — Wu Youru, 1886

Taiping Rebellion

1850–1864 — China

A failed exam-taker claimed to be Jesus’s brother, raised a peasant army of millions, and nearly toppled the Qing. 20–30 million dead.

Korea Chŏn Pongjun after capture, 1894 — “Green Bean General”

Tonghak Rebellion

1894 — Korea

Rejected Western civilization entirely. Created an indigenous religion to protect Korea’s soul — then peasants took it to war against corrupt officials and foreign powers.

Each movement occupied a different position on the spectrum of engagement with Western civilization. Japan said “copy their weapons, keep our soul.” The Taiping said “take their God, make Him ours.” Tonghak said “we have our own God, thank you.”

Why Did Japan Succeed?

The uncomfortable answer: it was an elite revolution, not a popular one.

  • Samurai leaders already had military training, administrative experience, and domain-level governing capacity.
  • Japan’s smaller size and relative homogeneity made centralization easier than in vast, diverse China or faction-ridden Korea.
  • The Tokugawa system had already produced a literate, urbanized society with proto-industrial infrastructure.
  • No religious vision to compromise with — pure pragmatism. They didn’t need to be right about God, they just needed to win.

The Cruel Irony

Japan’s “success” became Korea and China’s catastrophe.

  • A modernized Japan crushed the Tonghak rebels in 1894 with the same Western weapons Japan had adopted.
  • Japan used the Tonghak uprising as a pretext to send troops to Korea → Sino-Japanese War → Japanese domination of the peninsula.
  • The Taiping Rebellion’s devastation left China too weak to resist Japan’s rise.
  • Meiji Japan proved you could modernize — but it also proved that a modernized Asian power could be just as predatory as any Western empire.

The Class Divide

The most fundamental difference may be who led the movement:

  • Meiji: Led by samurai elites (existing military-bureaucratic class). They had the skills to run a state, not just overthrow one.
  • Taiping: Led by a failed exam candidate and joined by millions of desperate peasants. Massive military power but fragile governance.
  • Tonghak: Led by a marginalized intellectual (Ch’oe Cheu) and organized by peasant communities. Social reform agenda was genuine but no state-building capacity.

Revolutions from below can break things. Revolutions from above can rebuild them.

Religion as Revolution

Two of these three movements were fundamentally religious — but with opposite orientations:

  • Taiping: Hong took the foreign religion and weaponized it against the Chinese state. Christianity became a vehicle for anti-Manchu ethnic revolution and land reform.
  • Tonghak: Ch’oe Cheu created an indigenous religion specifically to counter the foreign one. “Eastern Learning” was the antibody to “Western Learning.”
  • Meiji: No religious content whatsoever. State Shinto came later as a tool of the government, not as a cause of the revolution. This may be a feature, not a bug.

The Question for Class

All three movements were rational responses to the same crisis — Western imperialism shattering the East Asian order. None of them were crazy. The Taiping saw that Western religion carried transformative power and tried to harness it. Tonghak saw that Western religion was the threat and built a spiritual firewall. Japan decided religion was irrelevant and focused on guns and railroads.

The question isn’t “who was right?” — it’s “what does ‘success’ even mean here?” Japan “succeeded” — and then colonized Korea for 35 years. The Tonghak “failed” — and their egalitarian ideals fed directly into Korean democracy. Hong Xiuquan “failed” — and Mao called him a forerunner of the revolution. History doesn’t grade on a simple pass/fail curve.
