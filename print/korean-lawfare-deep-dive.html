Lawfare: How Treaties Killed a Nation

The Legal Architecture of Korean Subjugation, 1876-1910
Japan did not simply invade Korea. It dismantled Korean sovereignty through a sequence of treaties, each signed under military coercion, each stripping another layer of rights, each dressed in the legal language of consent. This is lawfare: the weaponization of law. Five treaties across 34 years transformed Korea from an independent kingdom into a Japanese colony, all while maintaining the fiction that Korea had agreed to its own destruction.
The Ratchet of Dispossession
1876 Kanghwa Ports opened. Extraterritoriality. Tariff autonomy gone. →
1895 Shimonoseki Chinese protection removed. Korea "independent" and exposed. →
1905 Eulsa Foreign affairs. Diplomatic sovereignty. →
1907 Third Convention Internal governance. Army disbanded. →
1910 Annexation Statehood itself. Korea ceases to exist.
The Treaties
Treaty of Kanghwa (Japan-Korea Treaty of Amity)
Feb 26, 1876
Japan: Kuroda Kiyotaka (Special Envoy) · Korea: Sin Hŏn
Japan's opening of Korea, modeled directly on Perry's opening of Japan. The self-consciousness was explicit: Japanese leaders who experienced Perry's coercion in the 1850s reproduced the same template two decades later. In September 1875, Japan deliberately provoked an incident: the warship Un'yō entered Korean waters near Kanghwa Island, exchanged fire with shore batteries, and Japan demanded a treaty with warships anchored off the coast.
How It Was Coerced
Japanese naval squadron under Kuroda anchored off Kanghwa Island (mouth of the Han River, the approach to Seoul). Gunboat diplomacy, identical to Perry.
The critical provision: Article 1 declared Korea "an autonomous state (自主之邦) enjoying the same sovereign rights as Japan." Korea read this within the tributary framework (they already had internal autonomy under Chinese suzerainty). Japan read it as severing Korea from China entirely. Same words, opposite meanings. Japan was already laying the legal groundwork to isolate Korea.
Rights Stripped
Three ports forced open (Pusan, Wŏnsan, Inch'ŏn)
Extraterritoriality for Japanese nationals (above Korean law)
Tariff autonomy effectively surrendered
Japan's right to survey Korean coastal waters
Twenty Years Pass. Japan Defeats China.
Treaty of Shimonoseki
Apr 17, 1895
Japan: Itō Hirobumi, Mutsu Munemitsu · China: Li Hongzhang
This is the treaty Yi Kangnyŏn cites in his Open Letter. It ended the First Sino-Japanese War, triggered by competing interventions in the Tonghak Rebellion. Article 1 is the masterstroke of legal manipulation:
"China recognizes definitively the full and complete independence and autonomy of Korea, and, in consequence, the payment of tribute and the performance of ceremonies and formalities by Korea to China, in derogation of such independence and autonomy, shall wholly cease for the future."
Treaty of Shimonoseki, Article 1
Japan did not recognize Korean independence out of respect for Korean sovereignty. The entire purpose was to eliminate China's claim to suzerainty. Under the traditional East Asian tributary system, Korea was China's most important tributary state. As long as China could claim suzerainty, Japan could not freely dominate the peninsula. By forcing China to renounce all claims, Japan removed the only power that could legally justify intervention against Japanese expansion. Korea's "independence" was independence from China, which created a power vacuum Japan intended to fill. A legal clearing operation, not an act of liberation.
Rights Stripped
Chinese protective suzerainty removed
Korea isolated from its traditional security guarantor
Power vacuum created for Japanese domination
Japan Defeats Russia (1905). Western Powers Give Green Light.
Eulsa Treaty (Japan-Korea Protectorate Treaty)
Nov 17, 1905
Japan: Hayashi Gonsuke · Korea: The "Five Traitors" (을사오적)
The turning point. Before this treaty, Japan had cleared the field (removed China in 1895, defeated Russia in 1905). The Taft-Katsura Memorandum (July 1905) gave the US green light; the Anglo-Japanese Alliance gave Britain's. With every major power's acquiescence secured, Japan moved to formalize control. This is the treaty the reading's authors are most angry about.
How It Was Coerced
Itō Hirobumi personally pressured Emperor Kojong and each cabinet minister individually. Japanese troops under Hasegawa occupied Seoul and surrounded the palace. Military police (kempeitai) stationed inside the palace grounds during deliberations. Emperor Kojong refused to sign. Itō bypassed him and extracted signatures from five ministers.
Emperor Kojong never ratified it. He never affixed his imperial seal (御璽). He spent the next two years sending secret letters to foreign heads of state protesting that the treaty was void. The legal arguments: no imperial ratification, signed under military duress, ministers lacked authority without the sovereign's consent. The world did not care.
Rights Stripped
All foreign affairs placed under Japanese control
Korean diplomatic missions abroad recalled
Japanese Resident-General installed (Itō himself)
Korea ceased to exist as a diplomatic entity
Kojong's Last Gambit: The Hague (1907). It Backfires.
Third Japan-Korea Convention
Jul 24, 1907
Japan: Itō Hirobumi · Korea: Yi Wanyong (Prime Minister)
Triggered by the Hague Secret Emissary Affair. Emperor Kojong sent three envoys (Yi Sangsŏl, Yi Chun, Yi Wijong) to the Second Hague Peace Conference to denounce the Eulsa Treaty. Japan blocked them from being seated. Yi Chun died at The Hague on July 14. Japan used the affair as pretext for the final escalation: forced Kojong to abdicate (July 19), then signed this treaty five days later with the collaborator Yi Wanyong.
How It Was Coerced
Itō used the Hague affair as leverage. Kojong was forced to abdicate in favor of his son Sunjong on July 19. Under intense Japanese pressure, the new treaty was signed five days later. A supplementary memorandum (also July 24) ordered the disbandment of the Korean military, executed August 1.
The army disbandment is the critical detail. Roughly 7,800 Korean soldiers were dismissed. Many refused to disarm and joined the Righteous Armies (ŭibyŏng), the guerrilla forces whose voice we hear in Yi Kangnyŏn's Open Letter. This treaty is what turned scholars into soldiers.
Rights Stripped
Internal administration under Resident-General's direction
All laws require Resident-General's approval
Japanese nationals appointed to Korean government
Korean military disbanded (Aug 1, 1907)
Emperor forced to abdicate
Three Years of Formality. Then the End.
Japan-Korea Annexation Treaty
Aug 22, 1910
Japan: Terauchi Masatake (Resident-General) · Korea: Yi Wanyong (Prime Minister)
By 1910, Korea had no army, no diplomatic service, no control over its own government, and an emperor who was a figurehead installed by Japan. The annexation treaty was the final formality. Terauchi arrived with clear instructions to complete the process. Security was extreme: troops deployed throughout Seoul, public gatherings banned, newspapers suspended, nationalists surveilled.
How It Was Coerced
Negotiated in secret between Terauchi and Yi Wanyong. Other Korean cabinet ministers not informed until the last moment. Japanese military and police controlled every aspect of the environment. Signed August 22, publicly announced August 29.
"The Emperor of Korea cedes completely and permanently all sovereign rights over the entire Korean territory to the Emperor of Japan."
Annexation Treaty, Article 1
Korea ceased to exist as a sovereign entity. The Korean imperial family was given "appropriate treatment" (peerages, allowances). Korean law was replaced by a Governor-General with absolute authority. The nation was erased, with a signature.
Rights Stripped
Everything. Statehood itself.
The Hague Gambit (1907)
Korea's Last Legal Appeal
Emperor Kojong secretly sent three envoys to the Second Hague Peace Conference to denounce the Eulsa Treaty as void (signed under duress, without imperial ratification).

The Three Envoys:
Yi Sangsŏl (이상설) Yi Chun (이준) Yi Wijong (이위종)

Japan blocked them from being seated. Yi Wijong gave an impassioned speech at a side meeting. No government challenged Japan. Yi Chun died at The Hague on July 14 (the circumstances became a powerful nationalist symbol).

The affair backfired catastrophically. Japan used it as the pretext to force Kojong's abdication and sign the Third Convention.
The Five Traitors (을사오적)
Ministers Who Signed the Eulsa Treaty
Pak Chesun (박제순) Foreign Minister. Affixed the ministry seal.
Yi Wanyong (이완용) Education Minister. Later signed the Annexation Treaty too. The most reviled name in Korean history.
Yi Kŭnt'aek (이근택) Interior Minister.
Yi Chigyŏng (이지용) Military Minister.
Kwŏn Chunghyŏn (권중현) Agriculture Minister.

Their names became synonymous with treason. Emperor Kojong never signed.
The Western Complicity
Who Gave Japan the Green Light
Taft-Katsura Memorandum (Jul 1905): US acknowledges Japan's predominant interest in Korea. In exchange, Japan acknowledges US control of the Philippines.

Anglo-Japanese Alliance (Aug 1905): Britain gives Japan a free hand in Korea.

Treaty of Portsmouth (Sep 1905): Russia concedes Japan's interests in Korea. Roosevelt mediates, wins the Nobel Peace Prize.

The "rules-based international order" that Korea appealed to did not, in practice, apply to Korea. Every great power traded Korean sovereignty for strategic convenience.
In Today's Reading
The Documents Engage With Lawfare
The reading's authors knew exactly what had been done to them through treaties:

Yi Kangnyŏn (Open Letter): Cites the Treaty of Shimonoseki's guarantee of Korean independence as evidence of Japanese hypocrisy. "What the Sixteen Articles of the Treaty of Shimonoseki provide for?"

Declaration of Independence: References "the Treaty of Amity of 1876" and Japan's violation of "solemn treaty obligations."

Declaration of War (1941): "The 1910 treaty of annexation and all other unequal treaties are null and void."

Each document is a counter-argument to the legal fiction of Korean consent.
Still Unresolved
The 1965 Treaty Ambiguity
When Japan and South Korea normalized relations in 1965, the treaty stated the old treaties were "already null and void" (이미 무효). But Japan and Korea interpret this differently:

Korea: Void ab initio. Never legally valid. The annexation was illegal from the start.

Japan: Valid when signed, became void upon Korean independence in 1945.

A 2001 joint commission could not reach consensus. The disagreement persists and shapes Japan-Korea relations today.

The Logic of Lawfare

The five treaties follow a precise logic, each building on the last. Kanghwa (1876) pried Korea open. Shimonoseki (1895) removed China's protective umbrella. The Eulsa Treaty (1905) took Korea's voice in the world. The Third Convention (1907) took Korea's ability to govern itself. The Annexation Treaty (1910) took Korea's existence. Each step was dressed in the language of bilateral agreement, each signed by Korean officials (under varying degrees of coercion), each presented to the international community as a legitimate legal act.

This is what makes lawfare insidious. Naked conquest can be condemned. But when conquest comes wrapped in treaties, signed by ministers, and recognized by great powers, the victim loses not only sovereignty but the vocabulary to protest. When Korea sent envoys to The Hague to argue the Eulsa Treaty was void, Japan simply said: your country agreed to this; you have no standing to complain. The law that was supposed to protect Korea became the weapon used to destroy it.

The Declaration of Independence (1919) and the Declaration of War (1941) are both, at their core, attempts to undo the legal architecture of subjugation. The 1919 Declaration asserts Korean statehood in defiance of the annexation. The 1941 Declaration explicitly voids the treaties. Even the Provisional Constitution, by establishing a democratic republic, implicitly argues that sovereignty resides in the people, not in treaties signed by coerced emperors and collaborating ministers. Every document in today's reading is an answer to lawfare.
