Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 Is Lu Xun's critique of 'Chinese national character' valid or essentialist? Lu Xun was attacking specific behaviors he saw as products of Confucian culture. But 'national character' arguments can be dangerous—used to stereotype entire peoples. How do we read Lu Xun critically while taking his critique seriously?
2 How does Chinese nationalism differ from Korean nationalism? Korean nationalism emphasized victimhood and moral righteousness. Chinese nationalism (at least Lu Xun's version) emphasized self-criticism. Koreans blamed Japan; Lu Xun blamed China. Why the difference?
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 18: Plates 17-18 examined Japanese and Korean nationalism. Chinese nationalism has different character: more self-critical, more focused on internal transformation.
Context: Nationalism can be externally-focused (blame foreigners) or internally-focused (blame ourselves). Chinese nationalism had both strands. Lu Xun represents the self-critical strand.
To Plate 20: Plate 20 on Civil War and Revolution shows how this critical nationalism fed into revolutionary movements—Communist and Nationalist.
Notes
• Ah Q is darkly funny—let students laugh, then discuss why it's disturbing.
• Lu Xun's critique can feel harsh. Discuss whether self-criticism is necessary for reform or whether it becomes self-hatred.
• The 'spiritual victory' concept is useful for analyzing how societies deal with defeat. Can be applied broadly.
• May Fourth Movement is crucial context—iconoclastic questioning of everything traditional.
• Contemporary connection: Lu Xun is taught in Chinese schools but his critique can cut against official nationalism.
