Week 9 · Plate 25 · Friday March 6

Japan Rises Again

Eight Documents on Growth, Crisis, and Gender · 1960–1997
From consumer revolution to environmental catastrophe, from the income-doubling miracle to middle-class collapse, and from Hiratsuka's "I am the sun" to Ueno Chizuko's diagnosis of a feminism trapped between Orientalism and reverse Orientalism. These documents chart Japan's postwar arc: prosperity was never shared equally, and the questions about who benefits from "rising" were always gendered.

Part I: Democracy & High Growth
Document 1 · Government Report
White Paper on the People's Livelihood
Economic Planning Agency, Kokumin seikatsu hakusho, 1960 · pp. 1100–1102
The government's own breathless account of Japan's "lifestyle revolution." Tracks five forces behind the consumer boom: rising incomes (35% above prewar by 1960), the technological revolution (TVs, washing machines, refrigerators), a new mass of consumers from shrinking income inequality, a value shift from Tokugawa-era frugality to "enjoying life," and aggressive corporate marketing.
"Lifestyles have changed with special speed. In a word, this can be called Westernization. One can see this in eating habits . . . clothing habits . . . and housing habits."
Kokumin seikatsu hakusho, pp. 11–22
Ask: The White Paper names the salaryman household as the "emblem" of the lifestyle revolution. Who is missing from this picture? What does the government want to manage about this change?
Document 2 · Economic Policy
The Income-Doubling Plan, 1961–1970
Economic Planning Agency, New Long-Range Economic Plan of Japan, 1961 · pp. 1102–1105
PM Ikeda Hayato's signature policy. After the bruising 1960 Anpo crisis over the US-Japan security treaty, the LDP pivoted from revising the constitution to promoting economic growth. The plan targeted doubling GNP from ¥13.6 trillion to ¥26 trillion in ten years. It prescribed mergers, heavy/chemical industry investment (¥16 trillion for mining and manufacturing), and active state guidance of the private sector.
"Special effort must be made to rectify the existing disparity of living standards and income between farming and nonfarming population, between major enterprises and smaller business, between urban and rural residents."
New Long-Range Economic Plan, pp. 8–10
Ask: The plan promises equality alongside growth. Is this a welfare state or a developmental state? Compare to PRC five-year plans from Plate 23.
Document 3 · Protest Declaration
We Citizens: Sit-in Strike Declaration (Waga Shimin)
Minamata disease victims, c. 1971 · pp. 1105–1106
Mercury discharged by the Chisso Corporation devastated fishing families in Minamata City, Kyushu, beginning in the 1950s. The company denied responsibility and covered up evidence. This declaration from victims staging a sit-in at Chisso's Tokyo headquarters captures the emotional core of Japan's environmental movement. The title is a grim pun: shimin normally means "citizens" but is written with characters meaning "dead people."
"As we who suffer from Minamata disease and our families continue our sit-in strike in front of the company president's office at Chisso headquarters [in Tokyo], how can we possibly thank the many people throughout the nation who have provided us with their warm support?"
Waga Shimin declaration
Ask: The Minamata movement succeeded partly because economic growth made compensation affordable. Is there an irony in growth solving problems that growth created?
Document 4 · Management Memoir
Twenty Years of Labor Management
Orii, Rōmu kanri 20 nen · pp. 1111
An industrial manager reflects on how "scientific labor management" (modeled on Taylor) transformed Japanese workplaces. The "job surveys" aimed to collect objective data on working conditions. This is the management side of the postwar labor bargain: lifetime employment and company loyalty in exchange for rationalized productivity.
Document 5 · Journalism
"Farewell, Mainstream Consciousness!"
Yada Giichi, "Sayonara chūryū ishiki," 1997 · pp. 1111–1112
After a decade of stagnation following the bubble's collapse, Yada diagnoses the death of Japan's celebrated "mass middle-class" (chūryū) identity. Real estate plunging, stock values falling, interest rates near zero, temp work replacing lifetime employment. Nikkeiren reports that "flexible hires" are expanding while accumulated-skills positions shrink. Lifetime employees projected to fall from 80% to 70% of the workforce.
"How does one live in an era when the once buoyant consciousness of the middle-class mainstream is surrounded by fog?"
Yada, "Sayonara chūryū ishiki," p. 13
Ask: The consumer revolution of Document 1 created the middle class that Document 5 is eulogizing. 37 years apart. What broke?
Part II: Gender Politics & Feminism
Document 6 · Manifesto
Bluestocking & "New Woman" (Atarashii Onna)
Hiratsuka Raichō, 1911 & 1913 · pp. 1204
In 1911, Hiratsuka founded Seitō (Bluestocking), Japan's first feminist journal, "created for the first time with the brains and hands of today's Japanese women." Two years later, she defined the "New Woman" as someone who refuses the old morality built on male selfishness and seeks to create "a new kingdom, where a new religion, a new morality, and new laws are carried out, based on the spiritual values and surpassing brilliance of the sun."
"The new woman; I am a new woman.
I seek, I strive each day to be that truly new woman I want to be.
In truth, that eternally new being is the sun.
I am the sun."
Sievers, Flowers in Salt, p. 176
Document 7 · Scholarly Essay
"Are the Japanese Feminine?"
Ueno Chizuko, mid-1990s · pp. 1213–1219
Ueno, a sociologist at Tokyo University, dismantles the claim that feminism is a "foreign import" to Japan. Her central framework: Japanese intellectuals adopted Edward Said's Orientalism to describe Japan as "an oppressed woman" raped by the West. This "East-as-woman" metaphor traps feminists in a double bind. If they demand equal rights, they're accused of being anti-Japanese. If they celebrate feminine virtues, men respond: "We are already feminine enough." Ueno traces this cycle through Motoori Norinaga's Tokugawa-era celebration of kana as feminine/Japanese vs. Chinese/masculine, and forward through Takamure Itsue's maternalist feminism that slid into wartime nationalism.
"We find ourselves trapped in a vicious circle that oscillates between Orientalism and a reverse Orientalism. Feminism as a cultural product cannot escape from this closed framework."
Buckley, ed., Broken Silence, pp. 293–301
Ask: Ueno says the "dominant paradigm" stays intact whether you celebrate or reject the feminine. Is there a way out, or is this genuinely a trap?
Document 8 · Scholarly Essay
"What is Japanese Feminism?"
Saitō Chiyo, late 1990s · pp. 1219
Saitō, founder of the feminist journal Agora, offers her own definition: "An international cultural and human rights movement to 'cleanse' experience and knowledge, a movement that adopts a female perspective rather than the traditional male value system." She traces the word "feminism" to Alexandre Dumas's L'Homme-femme (1872) and argues the movement developed "philosophically out of a humanist tradition." Her definition pointedly refuses the nationalist framing that Ueno warned about.
Key Figures
Ikeda Hayato
池田勇人
1899–1965
Prime Minister
PM 1960–1964. After the Anpo crisis nearly toppled the government, Ikeda deliberately shifted LDP strategy from constitutional revision to economic growth. His Income-Doubling Plan became the blueprint for the high-growth era. Former Ministry of Finance bureaucrat who understood the levers of industrial policy.
In the reading: Income-Doubling Plan (Doc 2)
Hiratsuka Raichō
平塚らいてう
1886–1971
Feminist Pioneer
Founded Seitō (Bluestocking) in 1911, Japan's first feminist literary journal. Her "I am the sun" declaration became an iconic text of Japanese feminism. Later debated Yosano Akiko on state support for mothers (the "motherhood protection debate" of 1918–19). Became a pacifist activist after WWII.
In the reading: Bluestocking founding + "New Woman" (Doc 6)
Ueno Chizuko
上野千鶴子
b. 1948
Sociologist
Appointed professor at Tokyo University in the mid-1990s, one of Japan's most prominent feminist scholars. Known for work on gender, nationalism, and the "comfort women" issue. Her essay here uses Said's Orientalism framework to analyze how gender metaphors structure Japanese national identity.
In the reading: "Are the Japanese Feminine?" (Doc 7)
Takamure Itsue
高群逸枝
1894–1964
Historian / Feminist
Self-taught historian who traced matrilineality in ancient Japan, proposing the "maternal self" as a Japanese cultural ideal rooted in the goddess Amaterasu. Her countermodernist feminism rejected Western individualism but slid into wartime nationalism. Ueno treats her as the cautionary example of how reverse Orientalism leads to fascism.
In the reading: Central figure in Ueno's analysis (Doc 7)
Saitō Chiyo
斎藤千代
20th c.
Journal Editor
Founded the feminist journal Agora. Attempted to redefine "Japanese feminism" as a humanist movement free from the nationalist trap Ueno diagnosed. Positions herself in dialogue with Aoki Yayoi and Ueno Chizuko.
In the reading: "What is Japanese Feminism?" (Doc 8)
Yada Giichi
矢田義一
20th c.
Journalist
Wrote for one of Japan's two largest national newspapers. His 1997 article diagnosing the collapse of chūryū ishiki (mainstream/middle-class consciousness) became a touchstone for the "Lost Decade" era.
In the reading: "Farewell, Mainstream Consciousness" (Doc 5)
Key Concepts
Chūryū Ishiki
中流意識
"Mainstream consciousness." By the 1970s, roughly 90% of Japanese identified as middle class. This shared identity was central to postwar social cohesion. Its collapse in the 1990s marked the end of the high-growth social contract.
Reverse Orientalism
Ueno's term for the cycle where Japanese intellectuals adopt the Western Orientalist framework (East = feminine, passive) but try to revalue it positively. The dominant paradigm stays intact; only the value judgment flips. Takamure's maternalism is the feminist version.
Kokumin Seikatsu Hakusho
国民生活白書
"White Paper on the People's Livelihood." Annual government report by the Economic Planning Agency tracking consumption, lifestyle, and social trends. The 1960 edition celebrated the consumer revolution while worrying about the need to regulate it.

Synthesis: Who Benefits When Japan "Rises"?

The reading pairs two stories that are usually taught separately. The economic miracle narrative (Docs 1–5) tracks a clean arc: consumer boom, state planning, environmental costs, labor rationalization, eventual stagnation. The gender narrative (Docs 6–8) runs on a different clock entirely, with Hiratsuka's 1911 manifesto predating the economic story by half a century.

But the pairing is deliberate. The salaryman household that the 1960 White Paper celebrates as the "emblem" of prosperity depends on a gendered division of labor that goes unnamed in that document. The Income-Doubling Plan promises to close gaps between urban and rural, large firms and small, but says nothing about men and women. When Yada mourns the death of middle-class identity in 1997, the "secure" employment he means is male employment. Temp work, which he treats as a sign of decline, had always been "women's work."

Ueno's essay provides the theoretical key. The Orientalism/reverse Orientalism trap she identifies isn't just about feminism. It's about any attempt to define Japanese modernity against a Western standard. The Income-Doubling Plan measures success in GNP, a Western metric. The consumer revolution is described as "Westernization." The question "Are the Japanese feminine?" is really: "Can Japan be modern on its own terms?"

From the Bluestocking manifesto to the Lost Decade, the question was never just "Is Japan rising?" It was always "Rising for whom?"
