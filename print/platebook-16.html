Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 How does the film portray the Tonghak movement's motivations? Watch for: Religious elements, social grievances, anti-foreign sentiment, leadership dynamics. Is it primarily religious, nationalist, or class-based in the film's telling?
2 What perspective does the film take on historical events? Whose story is told? Are there heroes and villains? How are foreign powers portrayed? Is the government shown as legitimate or corrupt? Compare to what we learned from primary sources.
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 15: Plates 14-15 examined the historical Tonghak Rebellion and Joseon debates. Now we see how cinema interprets these events.
Context: Historical memory is contested and constructed. Film is one medium through which societies process their past. Compare to how Japanese, Chinese, and Korean films depict this era differently.
To Plate 17: After midterm break, Plate 17 examines Japanese nationalism. How do nations construct their identities through remembering (and forgetting)?
Notes
• The film is long (over 2 hours)—you may need to show selected scenes rather than the full film.
• Prepare discussion questions before screening to focus viewing.
• Have students take notes on specific scenes that struck them.
• Compare the film's portrayal to the historical readings—what matches, what differs?
• Discuss how films shape popular understanding of history, often more than academic work.
