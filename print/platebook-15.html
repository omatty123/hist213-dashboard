Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 Were the 'conservatives' simply backward, or did they have legitimate concerns? They worried that Western technology without moral foundation would be destructive. They saw Christianity as subversive. They valued social stability. In hindsight, some concerns proved valid—modernization did bring disruption and colonization.
2 Why didn't Korea have a 'Meiji Restoration' equivalent? Multiple factors: Stronger conservative faction, weaker reformist military base, different relationship with China, timing (opened later), stronger monarchy (vs. Japan's weakened shogunate). Avoid cultural essentialism.
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 14: Plate 14 showed how Tonghak Rebellion led to foreign intervention. This plate examines the intellectual debates that preceded and followed these crises.
Context: Korea's debates parallel those in China and Japan but with different outcomes. The spectrum from 'defend orthodoxy' to 'complete Westernization' was contested everywhere.
To Plate 16: Plate 16 is a film day showing 'Run High, Run Far' about Tonghak. Plate 17 begins examining nationalism across East Asia.
Notes
• The readings can be dense—focus on key arguments rather than comprehensive coverage.
• Avoid framing as progressive vs. backward; both sides had coherent positions.
• The Daewongun is a complex figure: isolationist but also reformist in domestic policy.
• Catholic persecution involved thousands of deaths—this was serious conflict, not mere policy disagreement.
• Connect to contemporary Korean debates about tradition vs. modernization, which continue today.
