Penetrating Questions Key Points
1 Ieyasu: "One who cultivates the way of the warrior only in times of crisis is like a rat who bites his captor" — how does this differ from Western heroism? Western narratives valorize improvised courage (underdogs, last stands). Ieyasu dismisses this as desperation. True mastery = constant preparation, not crisis improvisation.
2 Yamaga Sokō: "The samurai eats without growing, uses without making, profits without selling. What is the justification?" How do societies justify non-productive elites? Sokō's answer: moral authority. Samurai have leisure for ethical cultivation that busy farmers lack. Compare: clergy, academia, military in peacetime. Is it convincing?
3 The Buke Shohatto calls marriage alliances "the root of treason." Why was the shogunate obsessed with who married whom? Marriage = political alliance = potential coalition against Tokugawa. Control reproduction = control succession = control the future. Parallel: Habsburg marriages.
Connections
From Plate 7: Joseon Korea debated reform but couldn't implement. Japan took a different path: freeze society to prevent instability.
Broader theme: Both faced post-war order. Korea chose debate; Japan chose control. Which was "better"? Depends on what you value.
To Plates 10-11: When Western pressure comes (1850s), both Qing and Tokugawa face the question: can rigid systems adapt?
Teaching Notes
• The "rat" quote is gold — vivid, memorable, counterintuitive
• Sokō's "idler" anxiety is relatable — students facing job markets understand obsolete skills
• Frugality edicts surprise students: samurai = austere, not flashy
• Edo's size (1 million) shocks students — compare to London, Paris
• Compare to Plate 7: Korea debated, Japan imposed. Neither obviously superior.
