第二次世界大戦の人物 Who's Who: Japan at War

Nine voices from the documents. Know who's talking and why.
Plate 21 · EAST 213 · Feb 25

These readings are built from primary sources: speeches, propaganda, eyewitness diaries, newspaper reports, and radio broadcasts. Each document has an author with an agenda. Understanding who they are and what they wanted is the first step to reading critically.

Japanese Leadership

Prince Konoe Fumimaro

近衛文麿 (1891-1945)
Prime Minister of Japan
Document 17.1 — Address to Imperial Diet, September 5, 1937
Scion of the Fujiwara clan, Japan's most ancient aristocratic family. Served as Prime Minister three times (1937-39, 1940-41). His September 1937 speech to the Imperial Diet framed Japan's full-scale invasion of China as an act of "self-defence" forced upon Japan by Chinese "anti-Japanese sentiments." He later announced the "New Order in East Asia" (December 1938), the ideological blueprint for Japanese domination of the continent. Committed suicide by cyanide in 1945 rather than face war crimes prosecution.
"For one country to adopt as its national policy the antagonizing of and the showing of contempt for some particular country, and to make these the underlying principles of national education by implanting such ideas in minds of the young, is unprecedented in the history of the world." SMC, p. 316

Saitō Hiroshi

斎藤博 (1886-1939)
Japanese Ambassador to the United States
Document 17.2 — "The Japanese Ambassador Explains," 1937
Japan's ambassador to Washington during the critical years of the China war. His 1937 statement was aimed at Western audiences, especially Americans, to prevent US intervention on China's behalf. His argument: China's problems are self-inflicted, the conflict is China's fault, and Japanese rule would actually benefit everyone, including foreigners doing business there. He blames the fighting on Chinese troops violating the 1932 Shanghai truce. He even claims Chinese students are happily attending Japanese schools while the war rages. Died at his post in Washington in 1939.
"Who is to blame for the condition of China? Is it Great Britain, which sought for decades to help successive Chinese governments? ... Is it Japan, which almost staked her existence in a war with Russia to prevent 'the break-up of China'?" SMC, p. 317

General Matsui Iwane

松井石根 (1878-1948)
Commander, Central China Area Army
Documents 17.4-17.5 — The Rape of Nanjing, December 1937
Commanded the Japanese forces that captured Nanjing in December 1937. Before the attack, he called on the Chinese garrison to surrender, promising his troops would be "kind and generous to noncombatants." In reality, his soldiers carried out one of the worst atrocities of the twentieth century: six weeks of mass murder, rape, and destruction that killed an estimated 200,000-300,000 civilians. After the war, the International Military Tribunal for the Far East found Matsui responsible. Executed by hanging in 1948.
"Though harsh and relentless to those who resist, the Japanese troops are kind and generous to noncombatants and to Chinese troops who entertain no enmity to Japan." SMC, p. 324

Chinese Resistance

Chiang Kai-shek

蔣介石 (1887-1975)
Generalissimo, Republic of China
Document 17.3 — "Chiang Assails Prince Konoye's Statement," December 1938
Leader of Nationalist China and the Guomindang party. By December 1938, his government had been pushed from the coast to the inland wartime capital of Chongqing. This speech is a point-by-point demolition of Konoe's "New Order in East Asia." Chiang reads through the euphemisms and names them plainly: "economic unity" means economic control; "linked relations" means chains; "New Order" means annexation. He also invokes Sun Yat-sen's principled refusal to break treaties unilaterally, contrasting Chinese integrity with Japanese aggression.
"What is most urgent is that all should realize that Japan is determined to swallow China entirely." SMC, p. 320

Bearing Witness at Nanjing

John Rabe

约翰·拉贝 (1882-1950)
German Businessman · Safety Zone Leader
Document 17.4 — "Bearing Witness," December 1937
A German Siemens engineer who had lived in Nanjing for decades. When the Japanese army approached, most foreigners evacuated. Rabe stayed. As a member of the Nazi Party, he used his status as a citizen of Japan's ally to establish the Nanjing Safety Zone, sheltering an estimated 200,000-250,000 Chinese civilians. His detailed diary, hidden for decades, is one of the most important eyewitness accounts of the massacre. Often called the "Good Man of Nanjing" or the "Oskar Schindler of China."
"We had confidently expected that they would live up to their promises, at least in some degree, and that order would be established with their arrival. Little did we dream that we should see such brutality and savagery as has probably not been equalled in modern times." SMC, p. 326

Minnie Vautrin

"The Living Goddess of Nanjing" (1886-1941)
American Missionary · Ginling College
Document 17.4 — "Bearing Witness," December 1937
Head of the Education Department at Ginling College (now Nanjing Normal University), a women's college. During the massacre, Vautrin turned the campus into a refuge for nearly 10,000 women and children. She personally confronted Japanese soldiers at the gates, physically blocking them from entering to take women. In the "Bearing Witness" account, she is described kneeling on the ground with refugee women while soldiers searched them at bayonet point. The trauma of what she witnessed destroyed her. She returned to the US in 1940 and took her own life in 1941.
"Miss Vautrin was roughly hustled to the car ... He then demanded that we all leave, including the ladies, and when Miss Vautrin refused she was roughly hustled to the car." SMC, p. 327

The Killing Contest

Tokyo Nichi Nichi Shimbun, December 1937. Top: "百人斬り大接戦" (Great Killing Contest). Bottom: "百人斬り超記録 — 向井106–105野田" (Record-Breaking: Mukai 106, Noda 105).

Sub-Lt. Mukai Toshiaki & Sub-Lt. Noda Takeshi

向井敏明 & 野田毅
Imperial Japanese Army Officers
Document 17.6 — "The Nanjing Murder Race," reported in the Nichi Nichi Shimbun
Two junior officers of the Katagiri unit who competed to see who could kill 100 Chinese people with a sword first. The contest was reported as a sporting event by Japan's Tokyo Nichi Nichi Shimbun newspaper, complete with running score updates. When both exceeded 100 kills (Mukai: 106, Noda: 105), they extended the goal to 150. Mukai described it as "fun." The newspaper image shown here was used as evidence at their postwar trial. Both were executed in 1948. The casual, celebratory tone of the reporting is as revealing as the act itself.
"Mukai's blade was slightly damaged in the competition. He explained that this was the result of cutting a Chinese in half, helmet and all. The contest was 'fun,' he declared." SMC, p. 330

Collaboration

Wang Jingwei

汪精衛 (1883-1944)
Head of Collaborationist Government, Nanjing
Document 17.6 — Radio Address from Tokyo, June 24, 1941
One of the tragic ironies of the war. Wang was originally a revolutionary hero: a young radical who tried to assassinate the Qing regent in 1910, a close associate of Sun Yat-sen, and one of Chiang Kai-shek's fiercest critics after the Mukden Incident (1931). Convinced the war was unwinnable, he fled to Hanoi in 1938 and negotiated with the Japanese. In March 1940, he became head of a puppet "national" government in Nanjing. His 1941 Tokyo radio address is a study in self-abasement: praising the Emperor, thanking Japan for its "aid," and signing a treaty as humiliating as the Twenty-One Demands of 1915.
"Banzai for the Empire of Japan! Banzai for the Republic of China!" SMC, p. 333

The Communist Alternative

Liu Shaoqi

刘少奇 (1898-1969)
Senior CCP Official · Party Theorist
Document 17.7 — "How to Be a Good Communist," Yan'an, July 1939
Former Shanghai labor organizer and one of the CCP's most important ideologues. While Japan occupied the coast and Chiang's Nationalists held the interior, the Communists were building a parallel state in Yan'an, deep in China's northwest. Liu's 1939 lectures at the Institute of Marxism-Leninism aimed to transform millions of wartime refugees and new recruits into disciplined party cadres. Many were urban intellectuals from Shanghai, Tianjin, and Beijing, unaccustomed to party discipline. Liu became President of the PRC in 1959 but was purged during the Cultural Revolution and died in detention in 1969.

Reading These Documents

Questions to Carry Into Class

  1. Konoe and Saitō both frame Japan as the victim. What rhetorical strategies do they use to flip aggressor and victim? Where does the logic break down?
  2. Chiang reads through the euphemisms. Pick one of Konoe's phrases ("New Order," "economic unity," "linked relations") and trace how Chiang dismantles it. Is his reading persuasive?
  3. The "Bearing Witness" diary entries are day-by-day. How does the accumulation of detail work differently than a summary? What do you notice on the second or third day that you didn't on the first?
  4. The Nichi Nichi reported the killing contest as sports news. What does the tone of the reporting tell us about the Japanese home front's relationship to the war?
  5. Wang Jingwei's Tokyo speech vs. his earlier career. He was a revolutionary who tried to assassinate the Qing regent. How do you read his collaboration? Pragmatism? Betrayal? Coercion? Does it matter?
  6. Liu Shaoqi is building something while everything burns. While Japan massacres civilians and Wang collaborates, the CCP is training cadres. What does this tell us about how the war reshapes Chinese politics?
