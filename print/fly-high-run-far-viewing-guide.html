Fly High, Run Far — Scene Guide
Im Kwon-taek (임권택) · 1991 · 146 min · Read the Deep Dive →
A scene-by-scene companion for watching the film. Each card captures a key moment with the original Korean text, English subtitles, and historical context connecting it to course material. Guide grows as viewing continues.

Act I — The Founder: Ch'oe Che-u (최제우)

Scene 1
동학 1세 교조 · 수운 최제우 (水雲 崔濟愚)
Ch'oe Che-u Defends His Teaching
"No! I taught Self and Nature are the same."
On-screen text: 동학 1세 교조 = "Donghak 1st Patriarch." 수운 (水雲, "Water-Cloud") is his religious name.

The founder of Donghak, Suun Ch'oe Che-u, under interrogation. His core revelation (1860): humans and heaven are not separate. The concept of sich'ŏnju (侍天主, "serving the Lord of Heaven") meant that the divine dwells within every person. The state heard this as sedition: if heaven is in the peasant, the yangban order has no divine mandate.
●
Scene 2
燈明水上無嫌隙
Lanterns on Water: A Philosophical Exchange
"Lights lit brightly on water / leave no more space."
On-screen text: 燈明水上無嫌隙. Character by character: 燈明 (lanterns shine) 水上 (on the water) 無嫌隙 (leaving no gap, no shadow of suspicion). A line of classical Chinese (hanmun) poetry.

Im Kwon-taek regularly grounds the Donghak narrative in the Confucian intellectual world it emerged from. These scholars inhabit a world of literary refinement that Donghak threatened to democratize: Ch'oe Che-u taught that any person, not just the literate elite, could access the Way.
●
Scene 3
1864년 1월 · 대구감영 (大邱監營)
Trial at the Taegu Provincial Office
"You created heresy called Dong-Hak and deluded the public."
On-screen text: 1864년 1월 = "January 1864." 대구감영 (大邱監營) = "Taegu Provincial Governor's Office." The 감영 (kamyŏng) was the seat of provincial government in Chosŏn.

Ch'oe Che-u is brought before the court. The charge: creating a heterodox teaching (이단, idan, "deviating from the orthodox Way") and misleading the people. The formal accusation uses the label sahak (邪學, "evil learning"), the same term the Chosŏn court had applied to Catholicism decades earlier. Officials assembled on the upper gallery, soldiers below.
●
Scene 4
경상감사 서헌순 (慶尙監司 徐憲淳)
The Governor's Accusation
"They're said to be evil. Didn't you intend to instigate a treason?"
On-screen text: 경상감사 (慶尙監司) = "Kyŏngsang Provincial Governor." 감사 (kamsa, 監司) was the title for the governor of a province in Chosŏn administration. 서헌순 (徐憲淳) = Sŏ Hŏn-sun, the official presiding.

Note the costuming: full official regalia with amber court beads marking his rank. The question cuts to the core of the state's fear. Donghak was not just religiously heterodox; the government suspected it was a vehicle for political rebellion. In a neo-Confucian state, religious dissent and treason were the same category of threat.
●
Scene 5
1864년 3월 10일 · 수운 최제우 처형
Execution of the Founder
"He should have kept his head with magic!"
On-screen text: 1864년 3월 10일 = "March 10, 1864." 수운 최제우 처형 = "Execution of Suun Ch'oe Che-u." 처형 (ch'ohyŏng, 處刑) means execution by legal sentence.

Ch'oe Che-u is publicly executed in Taegu. The crowd's taunt is telling: they mock him for failing to use the supernatural powers the government accused him of possessing. Im films the crowd from behind, white-clad commoners walking toward the execution ground. The state's logic: destroy the leader, destroy the movement. It was spectacularly wrong. Ch'oe Che-u's martyrdom became the Donghak movement's most powerful recruiting tool.
●
Scene 6
방고 (榜告) · 경상도관찰사
Wanted: The Donghak Ringleader
On-screen text: 방고 (榜告, panggo) = "Public Proclamation," a formal government notice posted for public viewing. 경상도관찰사 = "Kyŏngsang Province Governor." The poster text (in classical Chinese): 此東學魁首 ... 捕縛此人者 以厚賞報之 = "The ringleader of this Donghak group ... whoever captures and binds this person shall receive a generous reward." Signed: 경상도관찰사 金一載 (Provincial Governor Kim Il-jae).

The hand-drawn portrait and brush calligraphy are period-accurate. After Ch'oe Che-u's execution, the government launched a decades-long manhunt for the remaining Donghak leadership, driving the movement underground. Ch'oe Si-hyŏng evaded capture for 34 years.

Act II — The Second Leader: Ch'oe Si-hyong (최시형)

Scene 7
동학 2세 교조 · 해월 최시형 (海月 崔時亨)
Haewol: The Sea-Moon Rises
On-screen text: 동학 2세 교조 = "Donghak 2nd Patriarch." 해월 (海月) = "Sea-Moon," his religious name. 최시형 (崔時亨) = Ch'oe Si-hyŏng.

Played by Lee Duk-hwa. Where Ch'oe Che-u was a visionary, Ch'oe Si-hyŏng was an organizer. He spent 34 years (1864-1898) building Donghak from a crushed sect into a mass movement with hundreds of thousands of followers. He published the movement's scriptures (the Tonggyŏng taejŏn, 동경대전), organized cell networks (p'o, 包), and extended the theology: his doctrine of innaech'ŏn (人乃天, "humans are heaven") radicalized the founder's teaching into a principle of absolute equality. Donghak later became Ch'ŏndogyo (천도교, "Religion of the Heavenly Way") and played a central role in the March 1, 1919 independence movement.
●
Scene 8
경주 용담정 (慶州 龍潭亭)
Yongdam-jŏng: The Founder's Village
"Dong-Hak founder's village."
On-screen text: 경주 (慶州) = "Kyŏngju," the ancient Silla capital. 용담정 (龍潭亭) = "Dragon Pool Pavilion." This is where Ch'oe Che-u had his mystical revelation in 1860 and founded Donghak.

Im Kwon-taek films the villagers crossing a stone bridge in white mourning clothes. The shot establishes Yongdam-jŏng as sacred ground: the place where Donghak was born, now bereft of its founder. After Ch'oe Che-u's execution, Ch'oe Si-hyŏng must rebuild from here.
●
Scene 9
영덕 (盈德)
On the Run: Yŏngdŏk
On-screen text: 영덕 (盈德) = "Yŏngdŏk," a county on the east coast of Kyŏngsang Province.

Ch'oe Si-hyŏng in flight. After the founder's execution, the government manhunt drove him into constant movement across the rural countryside of eastern Korea. The film tracks his decades of hiding through a series of location titles, each one a reminder of how precarious the movement's survival was.
●
Scene 10
차도주 강수 (次道主 姜秀)
Kang Su: Haewŏl's Right Hand
"Hae-Wol's aide, Kang Soo."
On-screen text: 차도주 (次道主) = "Vice Patriarch" or "Deputy Leader of the Way." 강수 (姜秀) = Kang Su.

The film introduces key figures in Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's circle. Kang Su served as an intellectual architect of the movement, helping to systematize Donghak thought and organize its growing network of followers. The traditional Korean house (hanok, 한옥) framing underscores the domestic scale of the underground: revolution begins in courtyards.
●
Scene 11
죽변 (竹邊)
Chukpyŏn: Planting a Persimmon Tree
On-screen text: 죽변 (竹邊) = "Chukpyŏn," literally "Beside the Bamboo." A coastal village in present-day Uljin County, Kyŏngsang Province.

Haewŏl plants a persimmon tree at his house, knowing he will almost certainly never enjoy its fruit. A fugitive who must keep moving, planting a tree that takes years to bear. It is one of the film's quietest and most devastating images: a man building permanence he cannot stay for. The gesture distills Donghak's entire wager, that you act for a future you may not live to see.
●
Scene 12
도인들은 조심스럽게 접(接) 활동을 재개하였다
The Cells Reactivate
On-screen text: Full translation: "The believers (toin, 道人, 'people of the Way') cautiously resumed their chŏp (接) activities." The chŏp was the basic organizational cell of the Donghak movement, a local gathering unit.

A wide landscape shot with a lone pavilion. The narration marks a turning point: after years of hiding, the movement begins to reconstitute itself. The chŏp network would eventually grow to encompass tens of thousands of followers organized across hundreds of local cells.
●
Scene 13
Theology from Below
"I'd rather be human than God. I prefer to be a woman."
One of the film's most striking lines. If Donghak teaches that humans are heaven (innaech'ŏn, 人乃天), then choosing to be human is not a lesser choice. And choosing to be a woman, in a rigidly patriarchal Chosŏn society, is a declaration that the most marginalized position is the most sacred one.

Im Kwon-taek centers women's experience throughout the film. This is not just a story of male religious leaders; it is a story of what revolutionary commitment costs the women beside them.
●
Scene 14
용담유사 (龍潭遺詞) 필사본 제작되다 · 구술
Transcribing the Sacred Texts
On-screen text: 용담유사 (龍潭遺詞) = "Yongdam yusa" ("Hymns from Dragon Pool"), one of the two foundational Donghak scriptures. 필사본 제작되다 = "manuscript copy produced." 구술 = "oral dictation."

Followers crouch over papers, copying by hand. Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's most consequential act as leader was publishing the Donghak scriptures: the Tonggyŏng taejŏn (동경대전, "Great Scripture of Eastern Learning") and the Yongdam yusa. Published around 1880-1881, these texts transformed Donghak from an oral tradition into a religion with a written canon. The shoes lined up at the threshold mark the scene as sacred space.

The Yŏnghae Uprising & Foreign Incursions (1866–1871)

Scene 15
The Byeongin Persecution, 1866
"In 1866, 9 French missionaries and 8,000 Catholics were executed."
Historical context delivered as on-screen narration. The Byeongin Persecution (병인박해) was ordered by the Taewŏn'gun (대원군), the regent ruling on behalf of the young King Kojong. Nine French Catholic missionaries and approximately 8,000 Korean converts were killed.

For Ch'oe Si-hyŏng, this was both a warning and a distinction: Donghak was founded explicitly as an Eastern alternative to Western religion. But the state made no fine distinctions. Any heterodox teaching was a target.
●
Scene 16
1871년 봄 · 영해 (寧海)
Spring 1871: The Road to Yŏnghae
"1871. Road to Young-Hae."
On-screen text: 1871년 봄 = "Spring 1871." 영해 (寧海) = "Yŏnghae," a district in present-day Yŏngdŏk County.

Two figures walk along a hilltop, framed against a bare tree. The cinematography is characteristic Im Kwon-taek: tiny human figures against vast Korean landscapes. The road to Yŏnghae leads to the first armed Donghak action.
●
Scene 17
이필제 (李弼濟)
Yi P'il-je: The Rebel
"Pil-Je Lee. The year before, he attempted a rebellion but failed."
On-screen text: 이필제 (李弼濟) = Yi P'il-je.

A militant Donghak follower who pushed the movement toward armed action, against Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's more cautious approach. Yi P'il-je had already attempted one rebellion before the Yŏnghae attack. His character represents the tension within Donghak between patience and militancy, between spiritual cultivation and political revolution.
●
Scene 18
1871년 3월 10일 · 영해부 습격
The Yŏnghae Uprising
"March 10, 1871. Dong-Hak attacked Young-Hae."
On-screen text: 1871년 3월 10일 = "March 10, 1871." 영해부 습격 = "Attack on Yŏnghae Prefecture." Note the date: March 10, exactly seven years after Ch'oe Che-u's execution on the same date. This was deliberate.

The Yŏnghae Uprising was the first armed Donghak action. It was a local affair, but it set a precedent: Donghak could be mobilized not just for prayer but for political violence. The consequences would shape the movement for the next two decades.
●
Scene 19
17개 군현에서 3백여 동학도 참가
The Scale of the Uprising
On-screen text: 17개 군현에서 = "From 17 counties (kunhyŏn, 郡縣)." 3백여 동학도 참가 = "Over 300 Donghak followers participated." 군현 (kunhyŏn) was the basic unit of local administration in Chosŏn: a county or district.

The numbers reveal the movement's reach by 1871. Drawing participants from 17 separate administrative districts meant the chŏp cell network was already functioning across a wide geographic area. Torchlight in the dark frames the scene as conspiracy.
●
Scene 20
미국함대가 5척의 군함과 1,200명의 병력으로
The American Expedition, 1871
"In 1871, American fleet attacked."
On-screen text: 미국함대가 = "The American fleet." 5척의 군함과 = "with 5 warships." 1,200명의 병력으로 = "and 1,200 troops."

The Shinmiyangyo (신미양요, literally "Western Disturbance of the sinmi year"): the United States naval expedition to Korea in 1871. The Americans demanded trade relations; Korea refused. The expedition attacked Kanghwa Island, killed over 200 Korean soldiers, and withdrew. For the Chosŏn court, this confirmed that Western powers were an existential threat.
●
Scene 21
열강의 침략에 대한 쇄국정책을 계속해갔다
The Hermit Kingdom Doubles Down
On-screen text: Full translation: "Continued the seclusion policy (swaeguk chŏngch'aek, 鎖國政策) against the invasions of the great powers (yŏlgang, 列强)."

A solitary figure walks through winter fields. The Taewŏn'gun's response to foreign pressure was total isolation: expel missionaries, reject treaties, fortify the coast. This policy shaped the environment in which Donghak operated. The movement was caught between a government that saw all novelty as Western infection and a population desperate for change.
●
Scene 22
1871년 8월, 문경 초곡 · 무기고를 습격하다 체포
Captured at the Armory
"Pil-Je was captured while raiding an armory."
On-screen text: 1871년 8월 = "August 1871." 문경 초곡 = "Mun'gyŏng, Ch'ogok." 무기고를 습격하다 체포 = "Captured while raiding an armory." 무기고 (mugigo, 武器庫) = weapons storehouse.

Yi P'il-je's final act: raiding a government armory at Mun'gyŏng. His capture and subsequent execution ended the first wave of Donghak militancy. For Ch'oe Si-hyŏng, the lesson was clear: armed revolt without sufficient preparation would destroy the movement. He chose patience, spending the next two decades building strength before the great uprising of 1894.

Rebuilding the Network

Scene 23
정석현 (鄭碩鉉) · 단양 서면 가산의 집
Shelter in Tanyang
On-screen text: 정석현 (鄭碩鉉) = Chŏng Sŏk-hyŏn. 단양 서면 가산의 집 = "House in Kasan, Sŏmyŏn, Tanyang." Tanyang is a mountainous district in Ch'ungch'ŏng Province.

After the Yŏnghae debacle, Ch'oe Si-hyŏng retreats deeper into hiding, sheltered by a network of sympathizers. Each location title in the film traces his geographic arc across Korea's mountainous interior. Chŏng Sŏk-hyŏn's house is one node in the underground network. The movement survives not through arms but through hospitality.

Opening to the World (1876–1881)

Scene 24
일본은 무력시위로 조선을 협박 · 병자수호조약을 체결하였다
The Treaty of Ganghwa, 1876
"In 1876, Japan forced Korea to sign."
On-screen text: 일본은 무력시위로 조선을 협박 = "Japan intimidated Chosŏn through a show of military force." 병자수호조약을 체결하였다 = "and concluded the Pyŏngja Treaty of Amity (병자수호조약)."

The end of Korea's isolation. Japan, having modernized after the Meiji Restoration, forced Korea open using the same gunboat diplomacy the West had used on Japan. For Donghak, the opening meant new threats (foreign economic penetration, missionary activity) but also new opportunities for a movement promising to defend Korean identity.
●
Scene 25
교세의 확장에 따라 경전간행의 필요성이 커지자
The Scriptures Go to Print
"In 1880 and 1881, Dong-Hak printed its scriptures for the first time."
On-screen text: 교세의 확장에 따라 = "As the movement's influence expanded." 경전간행의 필요성이 커지자 = "the need for scripture publication grew." 경전 (kyŏngjŏn, 經典) = "scriptures/canon." 간행 (kanhaeng, 刊行) = "publication."

The shift from manuscript to print was transformative. The Tonggyŏng taejŏn and the Yongdam yusa gave Donghak a fixed textual canon that could spread independently of personal networks. Followers no longer needed direct contact with leaders to access the teaching.
●
Scene 26
The Donghak Vision
"And all will live together in harmony."
A figure in white reclines on a reed mat, speaking the movement's utopian promise. This is the kaebyŏk (開闢) vision made personal: not abstract theology but a lived hope. No yangban, no commoner, no slave. Im Kwon-taek shoots the line in intimate close-up, not at a rally. The revolution begins in quiet rooms.

The Private Cost: Haewŏl's Three Wives

Scene 27
해월 48세 · 둘째부인 안동 김씨 맞이함
A Second Marriage at Forty-Eight
"You mean I need a new wife?"
On-screen text: 해월 48세 = "Haewŏl, age 48." 둘째부인 = "second wife." 안동 김씨 맞이함 = "married a woman of the Andong Kim clan."

Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's bewildered response reveals the human being behind the patriarch. The Andong Kim were one of Korea's most powerful aristocratic lineages. That a woman from this clan married a wanted religious fugitive speaks to how deeply Donghak had penetrated the social order.
●
Scene 28
The Children He Left Behind
"Children?"
"The youngest was adopted to a merchant."
"Nothing about the 2nd and 3rd child."
"Married to a government officer."
"And the 1st?"
A devastating five-line exchange shot almost entirely in darkness. Ch'oe Si-hyŏng asks about his own children and learns they have been scattered: adopted out, married off, lost track of. The close-up on his face when he asks "Children?" is one of the film's most powerful moments. Im Kwon-taek insists the audience see the domestic wreckage of revolutionary commitment. Donghak theology says every person carries heaven. These lost children carry their father's heaven, and he cannot reach them.
●
Scene 29
Haewŏl
Lee Duk-hwa as Ch'oe Si-hyŏng. The kat (갓, traditional horsehair hat) and white turumagi (두루마기, overcoat) mark him as a man of the yangban world he is dismantling from within. This portrait shot captures the gravity that won Lee the Chunsa Film Festival's Best Actor prize. The lattice screen behind him (ch'angsalmun, 창살문) recurs as a visual motif: a man perpetually framed behind barriers.
●
Scene 30
임오군란 (壬午軍亂) 이 일어났다
The Imo Mutiny, 1882
"In 1882, soldiers in Seoul..."
On-screen text: 임오군란 (壬午軍亂) 이 일어났다 = "The Imo Mutiny broke out." 신식군대 별기군과의 차별 대우에 = "due to discrimination in favor of the modern army, the Pyŏlgigun."

Old-guard Korean soldiers, unpaid for months while Japanese-trained units received preferential treatment, erupted in mutiny. They attacked the Japanese legation and the palace. The incident brought both Chinese and Japanese troops onto Korean soil.

Loss and Renewal

Scene 31
The Ancestor's Tablet
A spirit tablet (wiP'ae, 位牌) glows in the dark. Spirit tablets are the central objects of Confucian ancestor worship, placed on the family altar for ritual offerings. In the context of Donghak, this shot carries double weight: the movement honored ancestors while simultaneously challenging the Confucian hierarchy that ancestor worship upheld. The solitary tablet, lit against pure black, suggests both reverence and isolation.
●
Scene 32
1883년 3월 · 충청도쪽 교세가 커지면서
Westward Expansion: Ch'ungch'ŏng Province
On-screen text: 1883년 3월 = "March 1883." 충청도쪽 교세가 커지면서 = "As the movement's influence grew toward Ch'ungch'ŏng Province." 교세 (kyose, 教勢) = "religious influence/momentum."

The movement breaks out of its Kyŏngsang heartland into the central provinces. Poŭn (보은), in Ch'ungch'ŏng, would later host the massive 1893 Donghak assembly.
●
Scene 33
해월 61세 · 둘째부인 안동 김씨 사망
Death of the Second Wife
"His 2nd wife died when he was 61."
On-screen text: 해월 61세 = "Haewŏl, age 61." 둘째부인 안동 김씨 사망 = "Second wife, of the Andong Kim clan, died." 사망 (samang, 死亡) = death.

Im Kwon-taek gives the death of a wife the same formal title-card treatment as a treaty or a rebellion. The film's argument: the personal and the political are inseparable. Ch'oe Si-hyŏng kneels beside her in a darkened room.
●
Scene 34
보은 장내리 (報恩 帳內里) · 큰부인 손씨 처소
Poŭn: The Eldest Wife's Refuge
On-screen text: 보은 장내리 (報恩 帳內里) = "Poŭn, Changnae-ri." 큰부인 손씨 처소 = "Residence of the eldest wife, of the Son (孫) clan." 처소 (ch'ŏso) = "dwelling/quarters."

White-clad figures approach a dramatic rock formation. Poŭn would host the massive 1893 Donghak assembly. The landscape cinematography by Chŏng Il-sŏng makes the Korean countryside itself a character.
●
Scene 35
해월 62세 · 셋째부인 손씨 맞이함
A Third Marriage at Sixty-Two
"Hae-Wol married his 3rd wife when he was 62."
On-screen text: 해월 62세 = "Haewŏl, age 62." 셋째부인 손씨 맞이함 = "Married his third wife, of the Son (孫) clan."

Three wives across a lifetime of fugitive leadership. Im Kwon-taek gives each marriage the same formal title-card treatment as political turning points. The women who married a man hunted by the state were themselves making a radical political choice.

Toward the Uprising (1891–1893)

Scene 36
1891년 1월 · 호남 순회
The Honam Circuit
On-screen text: 1891년 1월 = "January 1891." 호남 순회 = "Honam circuit tour." Honam is the historical name for the Chŏlla Province region, Korea's southwestern rice-producing heartland.

A solitary figure on the edge of a vast field. Chŏlla would become the epicenter of the 1894 uprising. This shot marks the transition from underground survival to active mobilization: thirty years after the founder's death, the movement is strong enough to go on the offensive.
●
Scene 37
때가 왔네 때가 왔어 / 다시 못올 때가 왔네
The Rally Song
"Time is ripe. The time of no return."
"Long-waited hero will grasp this time."
"Why not use Dragon-Spring Sword?"
On-screen text (sung verses): 때가 왔네 때가 왔어 = "The time has come, the time has come." 다시 못올 때가 왔네 = "A time that will not come again." 용천검 드는 칼 아니쓰고 무엇하리 = "The Dragon-Spring Sword (용천검, 龍泉劍) is raised; why not use it?"

A torch-lit mass rally. The verses are from a Donghak mobilization song. The "Dragon-Spring Sword" is a legendary blade from Korean and Chinese tradition. The song's shift from spiritual patience to martial imagery signals the movement's radicalization.
●
Scene 38
Equality Tested: The Butcher Debate
"Never! You can't appoint a butcher to that position."
A gathering of Donghak leaders, shoes at the threshold. Can a person from the paekchŏng (백정) caste, the hereditary butcher class at the very bottom of Chosŏn society, hold leadership in Donghak? The outraged objection shows that even within a movement preaching radical equality, the reflexes of hierarchy die hard. One of the film's most honest moments: revolution is easier to preach than to practice.
●
Scene 39
Haewŏl's Answer
"God does not distinguish between classes."
Ch'oe Si-hyŏng settles the debate in one sentence. If innaech'ŏn (人乃天, "humans are heaven") is true, then it is true for the butcher. There is no asterisk on divinity. Not an abstract principle but a personnel decision. The close-up on Lee Duk-hwa's face carries the weight of a man who knows this will cost him followers.
●
Scene 40
1880년대에 들어와 서구 열강과의 조약에 따라
The 1880s: Treaties with the West
On-screen text: 1880년대에 들어와 = "Entering the 1880s." 서구 열강과의 조약에 따라 = "following treaties with the Western great powers." 서구 (sŏgu, 西歐) = "the West." 열강 (yŏlgang, 列强) = "great powers."

After the Treaty of Ganghwa (1876), Korea signed treaties with the United States (1882), Britain and Germany (1883), and others. Foreign goods flooded the market; foreign missionaries arrived; foreign troops appeared. The Korean countryside, where Donghak had its base, bore the economic brunt.

1893년 2월 11일 · 광화문 복합상소
The Kwanghwamun Petition
On-screen text: 1893년 2월 11일 = "February 11, 1893." 광화문 복합상소 = "Kwanghwamun Compound Petition." 복합상소 (pokhap sangso) = a joint petition submitted by multiple groups.

Donghak followers in kat and white robes sit in ordered rows before the main gate of Kyŏngbok Palace. They demanded the posthumous rehabilitation of Ch'oe Che-u and the legalization of Donghak. Black-robed officials on the steps above. The visual geometry is extraordinary: the petitioners' white against the officials' dark. This peaceful mass demonstration was a rehearsal for the armed uprising of 1894.

강수 (姜秀)
Kang Su
Vice Patriarch (차도주)
Haewŏl's intellectual lieutenant. Helped systematize Donghak thought and organize the growing network. Scene 10.

Yi P'il-je
Militant Rebel
Pushed Donghak toward armed revolt. Led the Yŏnghae Uprising (1871), then captured raiding a government armory at Mun'gyŏng. His failure taught Haewŏl the cost of premature action. Scenes 17-19, 22.

Narrative Arcs

Im Kwon-taek weaves five distinct storylines through the film. They overlap, contradict, and reinforce each other.

The Faith

Scenes 1-2, 7, 13, 14, 25-26, 38-39
The theological arc. From Ch'oe Che-u's revelation (sich'ŏnju, "heaven within every person") through Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's radicalization (innaech'ŏn, "humans are heaven") to the butcher debate, where abstract theology meets concrete social reality. The question: is equality a principle or a practice?

The State

Scenes 3-6, 15, 17-22, 41
Government persecution and Donghak's political struggle. Trial, execution, wanted posters, the Yŏnghae crackdown. Culminates in the Kwanghwamun petition, where the hunted become the petitioners, transforming from outlaws to a political movement demanding recognition.

The Underground

Scenes 8-12, 23, 32, 36-37
Thirty years of fugitive organizing. Location title cards track Haewŏl's movements across Korea's mountainous interior. Texts are buried and recovered, cell networks (chŏp) reactivate in secret, the movement expands westward from Kyŏngsang to Ch'ungch'ŏng to Chŏlla. Revolution as logistics.

The World Arrives

Scenes 20-21, 24, 30, 40
Foreign incursions punctuate the domestic story. The US expedition (1871), Japan forces Korea open (1876), the Imo Mutiny brings foreign troops to Seoul (1882), Western treaties flood the countryside with foreign goods. Donghak was born in an isolated kingdom; it reaches maturity in one being carved up.

The Human Cost

Scenes 13, 27-29, 31, 33-35
Im Kwon-taek's most distinctive choice: three wives, scattered children, a spirit tablet glowing in the dark. Each marriage and each death gets a formal title card, the same treatment as treaties and rebellions. The film insists that what revolution costs in kitchens and courtyards matters as much as what it wins on battlefields.

Key Terms Glossary

동학 東學
Tonghak / Donghak
"Eastern Learning." Korean religious and social movement founded by Ch'oe Che-u in 1860. Positioned as a native alternative to sŏhak (Western Learning / Catholicism). Wikipedia
교조 教祖
kyojo
"Patriarch" or "Founder of a teaching." Used in the film's on-screen text: 1세 교조 (1st Patriarch), 2세 교조 (2nd Patriarch).
수운 水雲
Suun
"Water-Cloud." Religious name of Ch'oe Che-u (최제우, 崔濟愚), founder of Donghak. Executed 1864.
해월 海月
Haewŏl
"Sea-Moon." Religious name of Ch'oe Si-hyŏng (최시형, 崔時亨), 2nd Donghak Patriarch. Executed 1898.
시천주 侍天主
sich'ŏnju
"Serving the Lord of Heaven." Ch'oe Che-u's original revelation: the divine is not distant but dwells within every person. The theological foundation of Donghak. Wikipedia
인내천 人乃天
innaech'ŏn
"Humans are Heaven." Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's extension of Donghak theology into a doctrine of radical equality. If every person carries heaven, then social hierarchy has no sacred basis. Wikipedia
개벽 開闢
kaebyŏk
"The Great Opening." Cosmic renewal; the prophesied transformation of the social order. Also the Korean title of this film. Film on Wikipedia
감영 監營
kamyŏng
"Provincial Governor's Office." The administrative seat of a Chosŏn province. The Taegu kamyŏng, where Ch'oe Che-u was tried, still survives as a historical park.
감사 監司
kamsa
"Provincial Governor." A high-ranking official (junior 2nd rank) who administered an entire province on behalf of the king. Chosŏn administration
방고 榜告
panggo
"Public Proclamation." A government notice posted in public spaces. Used for wanted posters, new laws, and official decrees. The one in the film offers a reward for capturing Donghak leaders.
사학 邪學
sahak
"Evil Learning." Label applied by the Chosŏn court to any teaching deemed heterodox. First used against Catholicism (sŏhak), then against Donghak.
양반 兩班
yangban
"Two orders." The hereditary aristocratic class of Chosŏn Korea, comprising civil and military officials. The social hierarchy Donghak theology fundamentally challenged. Wikipedia
처형 處刑
ch'ŏhyŏng
"Execution by legal sentence." Used in the film's on-screen text for Ch'oe Che-u's death: 수운 최제우 처형 (Execution of Suun Ch'oe Che-u).
천도교 天道教
Ch'ŏndogyo
"Religion of the Heavenly Way." The later institutional form of Donghak, reorganized in 1905. Central to the March 1, 1919 independence movement. Wikipedia
용담정 龍潭亭
Yongdam-jŏng
"Dragon Pool Pavilion." The site in Kyŏngju where Ch'oe Che-u received his revelation in 1860 and founded Donghak. Wikipedia
접 接
chŏp
"Contact" or "gathering." The basic organizational cell of the Donghak movement. Each chŏp was a local unit of followers; multiple chŏp formed a p'o (包, "pack"). Wikipedia
도인 道人
toin
"Person of the Way." The term used for Donghak believers/followers.
차도주 次道主
ch'adoju
"Vice Patriarch" or "Deputy Leader of the Way." The second-in-command under the Donghak Patriarch (kyojo).
용담유사 龍潭遺詞
Yongdam yusa
"Hymns from Dragon Pool." One of two foundational Donghak scriptures, written in vernacular Korean. Published by Ch'oe Si-hyŏng around 1881. Wikipedia
병인박해 丙寅迫害
Pyŏngin pakhae
"Byeongin Persecution." The 1866 mass execution of French missionaries and Korean Catholics, ordered by the Taewŏn'gun. Wikipedia
대원군 大院君
Taewŏn'gun
"Grand Internal Prince." The regent who ruled Korea 1864-1873 on behalf of the young King Kojong. Pursued radical isolationism and suppressed all heterodox religions. Wikipedia
신미양요 辛未洋擾
Shinmiyangyo
"Western Disturbance of the sinmi year." The 1871 US naval expedition to Korea: 5 warships, 1,200 troops. Repelled by Korean forces, it reinforced the Taewŏn'gun's seclusion policy. Wikipedia
쇄국정책 鎖國政策
swaeguk chŏngch'aek
"Seclusion policy" or "closed-country policy." Korea's isolationist stance under the Taewŏn'gun, banning foreign trade and missionary activity. Korea became known as the "Hermit Kingdom."
군현 郡縣
kunhyŏn
"County and district." The basic unit of local administration in Chosŏn Korea, below the provincial level.
무기고 武器庫
mugigo
"Armory" or "weapons storehouse." Government weapons depots were targets during uprisings.
병자수호조약 丙子修好條約
Pyŏngja suhojoyak
"Treaty of Amity of the pyŏngja year." The 1876 Treaty of Ganghwa, which forced Korea open to Japanese trade.
임오군란 壬午軍亂
Imo kunnan
"Military Disturbance of the imo year." The 1882 Seoul mutiny by old-guard soldiers against Japanese-trained units.
별기군 別技軍
Pyŏlgigun
"Special Skills Force." Japanese-trained modern military unit whose preferential treatment triggered the Imo Mutiny.
백정 白丁
paekchŏng
The hereditary butcher/leather-worker caste, the lowest social class in Chosŏn Korea. Donghak's claim that "God does not distinguish between classes" was tested by the question of paekchŏng membership.
광화문 光化門
Kwanghwamun
"Gate of Radiant Transformation." The main gate of Kyŏngbok Palace in Seoul, where Donghak followers staged their 1893 mass petition.
복합상소 複合上疏
pokhap sangso
"Compound petition." A formal joint petition to the king, submitted by multiple groups. The 1893 Kwanghwamun petition demanded rehabilitation of Ch'oe Che-u and legalization of Donghak.
호남 湖南
Honam
"South of the Lake." Historical name for the Chŏlla Province region, Korea's rice-producing southwest. Epicenter of the 1894 Donghak uprising.
