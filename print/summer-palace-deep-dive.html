Where Was the Summer Palace?

8 miles northwest of Beijing's Forbidden City ‚Äî the emperor's private retreat

The Garden of Perfect Brightness

The Yuanmingyuan (ÂúÜÊòéÂõ≠) ‚Äî the "Old Summer Palace" ‚Äî was not a single building but an immense complex of gardens, lakes, and palaces northwest of Beijing. Built over 150 years by Qing emperors, it covered 860 acres (five times the size of the Forbidden City) and contained some of the finest art collections in the world.

The gardens combined Chinese landscape architecture with European-style palaces designed by Jesuit missionaries. It was where emperors conducted state business during summer, where the imperial library was housed, and where priceless treasures accumulated over centuries were stored.

Western visitors who saw it before its destruction were stunned. One called it "the Versailles of China."

Why Burn It?

The burning was ordered by Lord Elgin ‚Äî James Bruce, 8th Earl of Elgin ‚Äî as deliberate retaliation. During negotiations to end the Second Opium War, a group of British and French envoys under a flag of truce had been seized by Qing officials. Of 39 prisoners, 21 died in captivity, some from torture.

Elgin wanted to punish the emperor personally without harming Beijing's civilian population. The Summer Palace was the emperor's private retreat, filled with his treasures. Burning it would be a blow to imperial prestige that would, in Elgin's words, "mark by a solemn act of retribution the crimes which had been committed."

"The destruction of the Summer Palace was an act of deliberate vandalism, meant to humble the emperor and demonstrate that no Chinese property was safe from Western power."

‚Äî James L. Hevia, English Lessons: The Pedagogy of Imperialism in Nineteenth-Century China

The Looting

Before the burning, British and French troops spent days looting the palace. Officers and enlisted men alike grabbed whatever they could carry: jade, silk, porcelain, gold, jewels, imperial robes, ancient scrolls. Auction houses were set up in the camp to sell the loot.

Captain Charles Gordon (later "Gordon of Khartoum") wrote: "We went out, and, after pillaging it, burned the whole place, destroying in a vandal-like manner most valuable property."

A British officer described watching soldiers smash what they couldn't carry: "Quantities of fine carved woodwork, beautifully lacquered furniture, and rich silks were ruthlessly cut to pieces or destroyed."

The Burning: October 18‚Äì19, 1860

On October 18, Elgin ordered the destruction. 3,500 troops from the 1st Division fanned out across the complex with torches. The fire burned for two days. The smoke was visible from Beijing, 8 miles away, for three days.

The French commander, General Montauban, had opposed the burning. The French had wanted to spare the palaces (and keep looting them). But Elgin insisted. The destruction became a British operation.

Only a handful of European-style stone buildings survived ‚Äî their ruins still stand today as the most photographed remnant of the complex.

What Was Lost

The destruction was incalculable:

  • The Siku Quanshu ‚Äî one of seven hand-copied sets of the largest book collection in Chinese history
  • Centuries of imperial archives and state documents
  • Buddhist scriptures, paintings, and religious artifacts
  • The famous Zodiac Fountain bronze heads
  • European clocks, musical instruments, and scientific instruments given as diplomatic gifts
  • Untold jade carvings, porcelains, silk robes, gold vessels

Estimates suggest 1.5 million objects were looted or destroyed. Many now sit in British and French museums. Others are in private collections worldwide.

Lord Elgin: A Family Business

James Bruce, 8th Earl of Elgin, was the son of Thomas Bruce, 7th Earl ‚Äî the man who removed the Parthenon marbles from Greece.

Father and son: one took Greece's treasures, the other burned China's. The British Museum holds both families' loot today.

Elgin later served as Viceroy of India and Governor General of Canada. He died in India in 1863.

The Prisoners

In September 1860, a diplomatic party including journalist Thomas Bowlby of The Times was seized under a flag of truce.

Of 39 prisoners, 21 died ‚Äî some from mistreatment, some executed. Bowlby's body showed signs of having been tied so tightly his hands fell off.

This became Elgin's justification. But critics noted: burning the palace didn't bring back the dead.

The Second Opium War

1856-1860: Britain and France fought to force further treaty concessions from the Qing.

Causes: The Arrow Incident (disputed ship seizure), desire to legalize opium trade, access to interior China.

Result: Convention of Peking (1860) ‚Äî more treaty ports, legalized opium, Kowloon ceded to Britain, indemnities.

Victor Hugo's Response

"One day, two bandits entered the Summer Palace. One plundered, the other burned... We call ourselves civilized and them barbarians. Here is what civilization has done to barbarism.

Before history, one of the two bandits will be called France; the other will be called England...

I hope that a day will come when France, delivered and cleansed, will return this booty to despoiled China."

‚Äî Victor Hugo, letter to Captain Butler, November 25, 1861

Qing officials board the Arrow, a Chinese-owned ship with British registration. Britain uses this as pretext for war.
