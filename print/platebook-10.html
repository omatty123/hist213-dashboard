"Let us ask, where is your conscience? I have heard that the smoking of opium is very strictly forbidden by your country; that is because the harm caused by opium is clearly understood. Since it is not permitted to do harm to your own country, then even less should you let it be passed on to the harm of other countries." — Commissioner Lin Zexu, Letter to Queen Victoria
Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 Was the Opium War about 'free trade' or drug trafficking? Britain framed it as opening trade; China saw it as defending against poison. Consider: Would we accept 'free trade' arguments for drug cartels today? But also: What alternatives did Britain have for solving trade deficit?
2 Why did China lose, and what does this tell us about comparative military strength? British naval and artillery superiority was decisive. But why was Qing military so weak? Internal focus, financial constraints, technological gap. Connect to Frank's argument: Was this the moment the 'Great Divergence' became visible?
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 9: Plate 6 showed Qianlong confidently rejecting British demands. Just 50 years later, China was forced to accept worse terms at gunpoint. What changed?
Context: The Opium War initiated a fundamental reordering of East Asia's relationship with the West. Every subsequent reform movement—Self-Strengthening, Meiji, etc.—responded to this shock.
To Plate 11: Plate 11 examines Japan's similar encounter with Western pressure—but with very different outcomes. Why did Japan avoid 'unequal treaties' longer?
Notes
• Read Lin Zexu's letter aloud—it's eloquent and makes a compelling moral argument. Then discuss why it 'failed.'
• The Treaty of Nanjing is short—have students identify specific provisions and discuss their implications.
• Avoid simplistic 'China victim, West villain' framing while acknowledging real moral asymmetry.
• Connect to contemporary drug policy debates—how do nations respond to drug crises?
• Show a map of treaty ports and areas of foreign control—visualize what 'unequal treaties' meant spatially.
