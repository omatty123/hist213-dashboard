Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 Was Wu Sangui a traitor or a pragmatist? He invited foreigners to conquer his own country. But his family was hostage; Li Zicheng had seized his concubine; the Ming was already fallen. He was rewarded with a princedom. Later he rebelled against Qing (1673-1681) and died fighting them. Loyalty to whom? Ming? China? Himself? His choice enabled conquest but may have been rational given circumstances.
2 Why did the queue matter so much that 60,000 people died at Jiangyin? Hair was a gift from parents in Confucian culture - not to be cut. The queue was a visible marker of submission to Manchu rule. Refusing meant choosing death over collaboration. Compare to other regimes marking bodies: tattoos, uniforms, identity papers. What makes some symbols worth dying for?
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 5: Plate 5 examined the Fall of Ming through internal crisis. Today we see how the Manchus exploited that crisis to conquer China - and how, 150 years later, the dynasty born in violence had become confident enough to dismiss European requests.
Context: These readings challenge simple narratives of 'Chinese decline' or 'Western rise.' In 1793, China was at its height - largest territory, huge economy, sophisticated administration. The Qing weren't backward; they were operating a different system. The question is why that system couldn't adapt to challenges that came after.
To Plate 7: Plate 7 examines Joseon Korea's reform debates. Like China, Korea operated within the tributary system and faced questions about engagement with outsiders. How did a tributary state navigate when the center was so confident?
Notes
• Read Wu Sangui's 'crying tears of blood' line aloud - the desperation is palpable. Then read Dorgon's cold, calculating reply offering a princedom.
• The Jiangyin numbers are staggering: 81 days, 60,000 dead, 75,000 Qing casualties. Ask students: was this heroism or futility? Both?
• Show the map with conquest locations (Shanhaiguan, Jiangyin, Yangzhou) AND Macartney locations (Canton, Chengde). Same empire, different moments.
• The queue question is great for discussion: what symbols would YOU die for? What makes resistance meaningful when it can't succeed?
• Connect conquest violence to Qianlong's confidence: the dynasty that killed 60,000 at Jiangyin now claims to 'possess all things.' How does memory work?
