The Unlikely General

He stood barely five feet tall, small even by 19th-century Korean standards. His neighbors in the farming villages of Jeolla Province called him nokdu - "green bean" - for his diminutive stature. Yet in 1894, this unprepossessing rural teacher would command the largest peasant army in Korean history, shake the Joseon dynasty to its foundations, and inadvertently trigger a war between empires.

Jeon Bong-jun was born in 1855 to a family of modest means in Gobu county, Jeolla Province - Korea's agricultural heartland and a region that had suffered generations of exploitation by corrupt officials. His father, Jeon Chang-hyeok, was executed in 1892 for leading local protests against official corruption. The son would finish what the father began.

녹두장군
Nokdu Janggun - "General Green Bean"

His soldiers gave him this affectionate nickname for his small stature. Far from being an insult, it became a symbol of how ordinary people could rise to lead extraordinary movements. Today, "Nokdu" mung bean pancakes are a famous dish in Jeonju, the city Jeon captured.

Jeon Bong-jun after his capture, December 1894. Seated center in white, flanked by Japanese and Korean guards. This is one of the few photographs of him. Note his small stature — his soldiers called him "Green Bean."

From Teacher to Revolutionary

Before he became a general, Jeon was a village teacher and practitioner of traditional medicine. He joined Tonghak - the "Eastern Learning" movement - in the late 1880s, drawn to its message that "human beings are heaven" and all people are equal before God. For a man who had watched his father die fighting injustice, Tonghak's combination of spiritual meaning and social critique was irresistible.

The spark came in January 1894. The magistrate of Gobu, Jo Byeong-gap, had forced peasants to build an unnecessary reservoir, then taxed them for the water from their own labor. When Jeon led a petition against this abuse, Jo had the petitioners beaten. On January 10, Jeon and a thousand farmers stormed the county office, freed prisoners, distributed hoarded grain, and demolished the hated reservoir.

"We are not rebels against the king. We rise to punish corrupt officials who suck the blood of the people, and to drive out the foreign barbarians who violate our land." — Jeon Bong-jun, Proclamation of the Peasant Army, March 1894

사발통문 (Sabal Tongmun) — "Bowl Proclamation"
The original circular calling peasants to rise. Names were written in a circle so no single leader could be identified as the instigator. This ingenious format protected organizers from execution.

The Green Bean's Campaign

What began as a local protest exploded into a regional revolution. By April 1894, Jeon commanded tens of thousands of peasant soldiers, armed with bamboo spears, farming tools, and captured government weapons. Despite their primitive armament, they defeated the government's professional soldiers at the Battle of Hwangnyongchon on April 7.

On April 27, the Tonghak forces captured Jeonju, the provincial capital. It was a stunning victory - peasants had taken a major city. Jeon established a headquarters in the governor's office and began implementing reforms: freeing slaves, burning debt records, redistributing grain to the poor.

But Jeon was no naive revolutionary. When the desperate Joseon government signed a truce (the Jeonju Peace Agreement of June 11), he accepted. The Tonghak forces withdrew, establishing "Jibgang-so" - peasant self-governance bureaus - throughout Jeolla Province. For a brief moment, it seemed that reform might come peacefully.

The Tonghak Rebellion: January - November 1894

Foreign Intervention and Final Stand

The peace was shattered by foreign intervention. The Joseon king had requested Chinese military help against the rebels. Japan, determined not to let China dominate Korea, sent its own troops. By July, foreign armies occupied Korean soil, and the war Jeon feared most - not a peasant uprising, but foreign domination - had begun.

In October 1894, Jeon re-mobilized the peasant army, this time explicitly to resist Japanese forces. It was a hopeless cause. At the Battle of Gongju in November, the peasant army - still armed largely with spears and clubs - faced Japanese troops with modern rifles and artillery. The slaughter was total. Jeon escaped, but most of his army was destroyed.

For two months, Jeon evaded capture, hiding in the mountains of his home province. He was finally betrayed by an informer in late December and arrested on December 28, 1894.

Profile

  • Born: 1855, Gobu County, Jeolla Province
  • Died: March 29, 1895 (executed, Seoul)
  • Father: Jeon Chang-hyeok (executed 1892 for protest)
  • Religion: Tonghak (Eastern Learning)
  • Occupation: Teacher, traditional healer
  • Nickname: Nokdu Janggun (Green Bean General)

Key Battles

  • Gobu (Jan 10): Initial uprising against corrupt magistrate
  • Hwangnyongchon (Apr 7): Decisive defeat of government forces
  • Jeonju (Apr 27): Capture of provincial capital
  • Gongju (Nov 1894): Final defeat against Japanese-backed forces

Tonghak Demands

  • Punish corrupt officials
  • Cancel unjust debts
  • Redistribute land to tillers
  • Free slaves
  • Expel foreign influence
  • End class discrimination

The Irony

  • Jeon wanted to protect Korea from foreign domination
  • His rebellion triggered the foreign intervention he feared
  • The Sino-Japanese War (1894-95) put Japan in control of Korea
  • By 1910, Korea was a Japanese colony

Legacy: From Traitor to National Hero

South Korea

Jeon is celebrated as a proto-democratic hero. The 1980s democracy movement adopted him as a symbol. Statues stand in Jeonju and his hometown. The mung bean pancake (nokdujeon) is named for him.

North Korea

The DPRK honors Jeon as a leader of peasant class struggle against feudalism and imperialism. His rebellion is taught as a precursor to the socialist revolution.

Historical Debate

Was he a revolutionary or a reformer? The Jeonju Peace Agreement suggests he wanted reform within the system. Foreign intervention, not rebel intransigence, prevented peaceful resolution.

The Tragic Irony

Jeon rose up to protect Korea from foreign domination. His rebellion triggered the very intervention he feared. Within 15 years, Korea was a Japanese colony.
