HIST/EAST 213: Modern East Asia

Reading the 1947 Constitution of Japan

A guide to what students should look for in one of the most consequential documents of the twentieth century, drafted in six days by American occupiers and reshaping Japan's political identity.

Promulgated November 3, 1946 · Enforced May 3, 1947

How It Happened: Six Days That Rewrote Japan

Oct 1945 Occupation begins

Matsumoto Committee formed

MacArthur orders the Japanese government to revise its constitution. The Matsumoto Committee, led by conservative Minister Matsumoto Joji, begins work. Their approach: keep the Meiji framework mostly intact.

Jan 24, 1946 Private meeting

Shidehara meets MacArthur

Prime Minister Shidehara Kijuro meets privately with MacArthur. MacArthur later claimed Shidehara proposed the war renunciation idea at this meeting. Shidehara later told friends the idea was his own. The true authorship of Article 9 remains debated.

Feb 1, 1946 Press leak

Matsumoto draft leaked

The Mainichi Shimbun publishes the committee's draft. Even its "progressive" version barely changes the Meiji Constitution. The emperor retains sovereignty. MacArthur concludes the Japanese government cannot be trusted to democratize itself.

Feb 3, 1946 The order

MacArthur's "Three Basic Points"

MacArthur writes three non-negotiable principles and hands them to Brigadier General Courtney Whitney: (1) Emperor as symbol, not sovereign, (2) total renunciation of war, (3) abolition of feudal aristocracy. Whitney assembles ~25 Americans to draft a new constitution.

Feb 4-12 Six days

The Government Section drafts the constitution

Nine subcommittees work around the clock. Colonel Charles Kades leads the Steering Committee. Beate Sirota Gordon, age 22 and the only woman, drafts the women's rights articles. She scours bomb-damaged Tokyo libraries for foreign constitutions as models. The final draft has 103 articles across 11 chapters.

Feb 13, 1946 "Atomic sunshine"

GHQ draft presented to Japanese officials

Whitney tells Foreign Minister Yoshida and Matsumoto their draft is "wholly unacceptable" and hands over the American version. As they wait in the garden, a B-29 flies overhead. Whitney remarks: "We have been enjoying your atomic sunshine." Eight months after Hiroshima, the threat is unmistakable.

Nov 3, 1946 Meiji Day

Constitution promulgated

Emperor Hirohito formally announces the new constitution. The date is chosen deliberately: it is the birthday of Emperor Meiji, linking the new order to historical continuity. Six months of preparation begin.

May 3, 1947 New Japan

Constitution takes effect

Japan's new government system begins. In the April elections under the new rules, 38 women won parliamentary seats and women's voter turnout exceeded 67%. May 3 is now a national holiday: Constitution Memorial Day.

What to Look For in the Text

This isn't a document to memorize. It's a document to read against what came before. Every article is a deliberate correction of something in the Meiji Constitution that the Americans (and many Japanese reformers) blamed for enabling militarism. Here are the key passages and what makes them significant.

Chapter II

Article 9: Renunciation of War

"Aspiring sincerely to an international peace based on justice and order, the Japanese people forever renounce war as a sovereign right of the nation and the threat or use of force as means of settling international disputes. In order to accomplish the aim of the preceding paragraph, land, sea, and air forces, as well as other war potential, will never be maintained."

This is the most famous article in the constitution and the most debated. No other constitution in the world contains such an absolute renunciation of military force. MacArthur's original note went even further: Japan would renounce war "even for preserving its own security."

The paradox: Japan today has the Self-Defense Forces (SDF), one of the world's most capable militaries. Successive governments have reinterpreted Article 9 to permit defensive capability. The tension between the text and reality has been central to Japanese politics for 75+ years.

Ask yourself: Who does this article serve? MacArthur's strategic interests? Japanese pacifist ideals? Both?

Chapter I

Articles 1-8: The Emperor

"The Emperor shall be the symbol of the State and of the unity of the people, deriving his position from the will of the people with whom resides sovereign power."

Under the Meiji Constitution, the emperor held supreme political power by divine right ("sacred and inviolable," "lineal succession unbroken for ages eternal"). He commanded the military directly, independent of the Diet. He could declare war, conclude treaties, and dissolve parliament.

The 1947 constitution strips all political power. The emperor becomes a "symbol." His ceremonial duties require cabinet approval. This was MacArthur's compromise: keep the emperor to ensure stability (and avoid prosecution as a war criminal), but remove every scrap of governing authority.

Notice the language: "deriving his position from the will of the people." Where did his position derive from before?

Chapter III

Articles 10-40: Rights and Duties of the People

This is the longest chapter, and it reads like a catalog of everything the Meiji Constitution did not guarantee. Under Meiji, rights were granted by the emperor and could be limited by law. Here, rights are described as "eternal and inviolate" (Art. 11) and "the supreme consideration in legislation" (Art. 13).

Key articles to read closely:

Article 14 (equality): Prohibits discrimination by "race, creed, sex, social status or family origin." Abolishes peerage. Drafted by 22-year-old Beate Sirota Gordon.

Article 19 (thought): "Freedom of thought and conscience shall not be violated." One sentence. Absolute.

Article 24 (marriage): Requires "mutual consent of both sexes" and "equal rights of husband and wife." Revolutionary in 1946 Japan. Also drafted by Sirota Gordon.

Articles 31-40 (criminal protections): Right to counsel, ban on torture, ban on forced confessions, double jeopardy protection. Read these against Japan's wartime security apparatus.

How many of these articles echo the U.S. Bill of Rights? What does that tell you about who wrote this document?

Preamble

The Preamble: Whose Voice?

"We, the Japanese people, acting through our duly elected representatives in the National Diet, determined that we shall secure for ourselves and our posterity the fruits of peaceful cooperation with all nations..."

The preamble begins "We, the Japanese people." But the Japanese people did not write it. Twenty-five Americans in a military headquarters did, in six days, in English. The Japanese government was then presented with it under implicit threat.

This tension has never fully resolved. Conservative critics call it an "imposed constitution" (oshitsuke kenpo). Supporters argue it reflects genuine Japanese aspirations that the wartime government had suppressed. The fact that Japan has never amended it, despite decades of debate, suggests something more complex than pure imposition.

Read the preamble out loud. Does it sound like it was written by Japanese people? What phrases feel American?

Chapters IV-VI

The Government Structure

The structural changes are less dramatic to read but equally important:

The Diet (Art. 41): "The highest organ of state power." Not the emperor, not the military. The parliament. And cabinet ministers "must be civilians" (Art. 66), a direct response to the military officers who dominated prewar cabinets.

Judiciary (Art. 76-82): An independent Supreme Court with the power of judicial review (Art. 81). Under Meiji, courts had no power to strike down laws. This is another American import.

Local government (Chapter VIII): A new concept. Meiji Japan was rigidly centralized. The 1947 constitution creates local autonomy (Art. 92-95), a deliberate effort to prevent the concentration of power that enabled militarism.

Why would the American drafters care so much about decentralization? What are they trying to prevent?

Article 97, "Supreme Law"
The Beate Sirota Gordon Story

Age 22. The only woman among the drafters.

Born in Vienna to a Ukrainian pianist father. Grew up in Tokyo from age 5 while her father taught at the Imperial Academy of Music. Fluent in Japanese.

Assigned to the Civil Rights subcommittee, she drafted Articles 14 (equality) and 24 (marriage equality). She personally scoured bomb-damaged Tokyo libraries for foreign constitutions as models.

Her original proposals included even more protections for women. Some were cut by her superiors. She kept her role secret for nearly 50 years, going public only in 1995.

Her articles remain unamended.

The Article 9 Debate

Who actually proposed the war renunciation clause? Three theories:

MacArthur's initiative: His Feb 3 notes contain the language. Strategic motive: protect the emperor from war crimes prosecution by making Japan permanently peaceful.

Shidehara's idea: MacArthur claimed PM Shidehara proposed it on Jan 24. Shidehara later told friends it was his own idea. His career as a dovish diplomat lends credibility.

Kades shaped the final text: The Government Section's actual drafting, and later Diet negotiations, determined the exact language.

Recent scholarship gives increasing weight to Shidehara's role in the concept, with the Americans formalizing it into law.

Key Question

The 1947 constitution was technically enacted as an "amendment" to the Meiji Constitution (using its own Article 73 amendment procedure). But it replaced every fundamental principle.

Is this a revolution disguised as a revision?

And if so, what does it mean that the revolution was written by foreign occupiers in a language the Japanese public couldn't read?

Still Unamended

The 1947 constitution has never been amended. Not once. In nearly 80 years.

The U.S. Constitution has been amended 27 times. Germany's Basic Law, over 60 times.

Conservative parties have sought to revise Article 9 for decades. The Liberal Democratic Party published a formal revision draft in 2012. Public opinion remains divided.

The constitution's durability is itself a historical puzzle: imposed by foreigners, written in English, and yet Japanese citizens have chosen to keep every word.
