Penetrating Questions Key Points
1 Lee visited the Korean Provisional Government site in Shanghai. Why does colonial-era history shape Korean diplomacy in 2026? KPG (1919-1945) = Korean resistance to Japan AND independence from China. Lee signals: Korea has its own identity forged in struggle. Compare to Joseon reformers (Plate 7).
2 China recalled pandas from Japan. What does weaponizing "soft power" tell us about modern great-power competition? Economic interdependence = vulnerability. Export controls, rare earths = modern gunboat diplomacy. Pandas as carrot AND stick. Compare Qianlong's confidence (Plate 6) to Opium War (Plate 10).
3 North Korea fired missiles the exact moment Lee arrived in Beijing. What does this tell us about how division shapes current politics? Pyongyang signaling it won't be managed by China-SK cooperation. Korean War never ended—longest "frozen conflict." Division as unfinished WWII/Cold War business.
Connections
From Plates 1-8: East Asian cultural sphere, tributary relationships, reform debates, regional warfare. Today's headlines show these patterns remain active.
Broader theme: Historical continuity — Korea's middle-power dilemma, Japan's security transformation, Taiwan's ambiguous status all rooted in dynamics we've studied.
To Plate 10: Qing vs. the West. From Qianlong's confident letter to "unequal treaties." Origins of China's "century of humiliation" narrative.
Teaching Notes
