"When order is at its peak, chaos results; when chaos is at its peak, order results." — Song Yingxing, 'The Trend of the World'
Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 Why did ordinary people sometimes prefer rebel leaders like Li Zicheng to Ming government forces? Both rebel and government armies looted and killed; Li promised relief from taxes and discipline; 'the people looked upon the bandits as their benefactors'; government troops drove people to rebellion
2 Was Shi Kefa's martyrdom at Yangzhou morally justified, given that his resistance led to the massacre of tens of thousands of civilians? Loyalist duty vs. responsibility to civilians; Dodo explicitly used Yangzhou as 'example' for other cities; did other cities surrender faster because of the massacre?; ethics of resistance when civilians bear the cost
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 4: Plate 4 covered the Imjin War (1592-1598) when Japan invaded Korea. The Ming's costly intervention to save Joseon weakened imperial finances - setting the stage for the fiscal crisis and military weakness that enabled both the internal rebellions and Manchu conquest covered in Plate 5.
Context: The Ming-Qing transition exemplifies the course's central themes: how internal crisis enables external conquest; how 'modernity' often arrives through violence; how ordinary people navigate between competing authorities; how cultural identity becomes politicized during regime change.
To Plate 6: Plate 6 will examine Qing's first major encounter with the West through the Macartney Mission (1793). Students should note how the confident, assertive Qing state that emerges from conquest will later struggle to adapt to Western pressure.
Notes
• Students may confuse the many rebel leaders - emphasize the Li Zicheng (ideological) vs. Zhang Xianzhong (terror) contrast
• The queue decree often surprises students - connect to modern examples of forced cultural assimilation
• Shi Kefa's expectation that his wife die may disturb students - use as entry point for discussing gender and loyalty norms
• The 'chaos/order' cyclical view contrasts with linear Western notions of progress - good discussion point
• Consider showing a map of the conquest (Beijing -> Nanjing -> Yangzhou) to help students visualize the Manchu advance
