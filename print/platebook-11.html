Penetrating Questions Key Points
1 Nariaki: "The greatest disgrace since the dawn of our history." Ii Naosuke: "To raise the drawbridge is to imprison oneself." Who was right? Both were patriots with different strategic assessments. Nariaki: honor and morale. Ii Naosuke: survival and adaptation. History vindicated Ii Naosuke—but only after armed resistance failed (1864).
2 The Harris Treaty banned opium. Does this mean Japan negotiated "better" than China? No. The US didn't trade opium; Britain did. Japan still accepted extraterritoriality, fixed tariffs, MFN clauses. Different aggressor, different commodities—not Japanese cleverness vs. Chinese failure.
3 Ii Naosuke proposed building warships disguised as "merchant vessels" to secretly train a navy. Is such deception justified? "Strategic opening": engage to strengthen, then compete. Ii acknowledged he was advocating revolutionary change—his views "conflict with existing laws."
Connections
From Plate 10: China's opening via Opium War. Japan faced similar pressure with key differences: 15 years warning, no concurrent civil war, Americans wanted ports not drugs.
Broader theme: Both signed "unequal treaties." Reject "Japan succeeded, China failed" framing. Better: What obstacles did each face in responding?
To Plate 12: Meiji Transformation. "Learn from the West" triumphed—but through Satsuma/Choshu, who overthrew Tokugawa entirely.
Teaching Notes
• KEY DEBATE: Have students argue Nariaki vs. Ii Naosuke positions
• Ii Naosuke's assassination (1860) shows debate wasn't just rhetorical—people died
• "Secret navy" proposal shows sophisticated strategic thinking—still relevant today
• Nariaki's 10 REASONS: national prestige, Christianity threat, trade drains resources, precedent, foothold leads to more demands...
