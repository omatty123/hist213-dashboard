Film Study Guide · Day 16
개벽
FLY HIGH, RUN FAR
Directed by Im Kwon-taek (임권택) · 1991
"A beautiful and powerful spiritual epic… charting the growth and eventual stamping out of a radically humanist and egalitarian religious sect founded on the belief that God is everyone and everything."
Watch the Film

About the Film

Fly High, Run Far (Korean title: 개벽, Gaebyeok) is Im Kwon-taek's dramatization of the Donghak movement as seen through the life of its second patriarch, Ch'oe Si-hyŏng (1827–1898). The film follows Ch'oe from his impoverished origins as a farmhand to his rise as leader of the movement after its founder Ch'oe Che-u was executed by the Chosŏn government in 1864. Across nearly two and a half hours, the film charts the spread of Donghak through underground networks, the mobilization of tens of thousands of followers, the 1894 peasant uprising, and Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's eventual arrest and execution in 1898.

The screenplay by Kim Yong-ok treats Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's story with the sweep of a historical epic, but as critic Jonathan Rosenbaum noted, "the film is anything but slow or ponderous." It interweaves political crisis with intimate domestic drama, focusing especially on Ch'oe's relationships with his three wives and the personal sacrifices demanded by his role as leader of a persecuted religious movement.

In the film, Ch'oe Si-hyŏng is called by his religious name, Hae-wol (海月, "Sea-Moon"), played by Lee Duk-hwa in a performance that won the Chunsa Film Festival's Best Actor prize.

The Korean Title: 개벽

Gaebyeok (開闢) is not just the title of this film. It is one of the central theological concepts in Donghak thought, a word that carries enormous weight. Derived from the classical Chinese expression ch'ŏn'gae chibyŏk (天開地闢, "heaven opens, earth opens"), it refers to a cosmic renewal: the Great Opening of a new era.

For Donghak, this was not abstract metaphysics. Ch'oe Che-u prophesied the huch'ŏn gaebyeok (後天開闢), the "Great Opening of the Later Heaven," a coming transformation in which the corrupt hierarchies of the current world would be swept away and replaced by a society grounded in radical equality. Title the film Gaebyeok and you are announcing: this is a story about the attempt to remake the world from the ground up.

"A radically humanist and egalitarian religious sect founded on the belief that God is everyone and everything." Jonathan Rosenbaum, Chicago Reader

Donghak Theology: Three Core Concepts

The film operates within a theological framework that would have been deeply familiar to Korean audiences but may require some unpacking. Three interlocking concepts drive the action.

侍天主
Sich'ŏnju
"Serving Heaven as Master." The divine is not distant; it dwells within every person. This was Ch'oe Che-u's original revelation in 1860.
人乃天
Innaech'ŏn
"Humans are Heaven." Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's extension of the founder's teaching into a principle of radical equality.
開闢
Gaebyeok
"The Great Opening." The prophecy of cosmic renewal and a new social order.

The political implications were explosive. If heaven dwells in every person, then the rigid hierarchies of Chosŏn society (yangban over commoner, man over woman, old over young) have no divine sanction. Ch'oe Si-hyŏng translated this theology into social practice by teaching that people should "serve man as they serve heaven" (sain yŏch'ŏn, 事人如天). Beating a child or mistreating a servant was an offense against the divine.

The Chosŏn court understood perfectly well what this meant. A religion that declared farmhands equal to aristocrats was a political threat regardless of whether its followers carried weapons.

Ch'oe Si-hyŏng: The Historical Figure

Origins

Ch'oe Si-hyŏng was born in 1827 in Kyŏngju, Kyŏngsang Province. His mother died when he was six; his father when he was fifteen. He worked as a farmhand and papermaker. He encountered Ch'oe Che-u and the Donghak movement in 1861 and became an ardent participant in ritual chanting, fasting, and penance.

Building the Underground

After Ch'oe Che-u's execution in 1864, Ch'oe Si-hyŏng spent the next three decades building Donghak from a persecuted sect into a mass movement. He published the first two Donghak scriptures in 1880–1881, giving the religion an intellectual foundation. He organized the movement into a network of local cells (p'o) that could survive government suppression.

Mass Mobilization

In 1892, he mobilized tens of thousands of followers for a peaceful demonstration at Kongju and then at the Kwanghwamun gate in Seoul, demanding rehabilitation of the founder's name, religious freedom, and administrative reform. The banner read: "Expel the West, Expel the Japanese, and Inculcate Righteousness."

The Uprising and Its Aftermath

The 1894 Donghak Peasant Revolution was a complex event. Chŏn Pong-jun led the armed uprising in Chŏlla Province, while Ch'oe Si-hyŏng initially hesitated before committing the northern Donghak forces. The uprising was crushed with the help of Japanese and Qing troops. Ch'oe Si-hyŏng evaded capture for four more years before his arrest and execution in Seoul in 1898. By then, Donghak had embedded itself throughout Korea. It later became Ch'ŏndogyo ("Religion of the Heavenly Way") and played a central role in the March 1, 1919 independence movement.

Im Kwon-taek and Korean Cinema

Understanding this film means understanding its director. Im Kwon-taek (born 1936 in Changsŏng, South Chŏlla Province) is the most prolific and arguably the most important filmmaker in Korean cinema history. He has directed over 102 films since his debut with Farewell to the Duman River in 1962.

His early career was commercial genre work: action films, melodramas, quick productions. The turn came in the mid-1970s when he began making films that explored Korean cultural identity, historical trauma, and traditional arts with the seriousness and ambition of an auteur.

Fly High, Run Far sits at the hinge point of Im's career, between the Buddhist films of the 1980s and the cultural masterpieces of the 1990s. The Donghak movement gave Im material that combined his interests in Korean spirituality, social justice, and historical trauma into a single narrative. Critic Jonathan Rosenbaum included the film on his Ten Best Films list for 1990–1994, a striking endorsement from one of American cinema's most rigorous voices. The ICA (Institute of Contemporary Arts) in London named an entire retrospective season after the film: "Im Kwon-taek: Run Far, Fly High." MoMA held a 15-film Im Kwon-taek retrospective in 2004.

The scholarly volume Im Kwon-Taek: The Making of a Korean National Cinema (edited by David E. James and Kyung Hyun Kim, Wayne State University Press) includes a dedicated chapter on the film by Yi Hyoin: "Fly High, Run Far: Kaebyeok and Tonghak Ideology." Yi argues that of all Im's films, this one "comes closest to portraying Im's own views on life."

What to Watch For

The Domestic Sphere

Pay attention to Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's three wives. Im Kwon-taek does not simply tell a story about a religious leader; he shows what religious leadership costs the people closest to the leader. The private dimension of revolutionary commitment is one of the film's most distinctive features.

Printed Titles

The film uses on-screen text to provide historical context at key moments. This is a deliberate formal choice, treating the film almost like a historical pageant or illustrated chronicle. It keeps the audience oriented in a complex political timeline.

Class and the Body

Donghak's theology of radical equality means that bodies matter. Watch how the film frames physical labor, physical suffering, and physical punishment. The doctrine of innaech'ŏn says that violence against any human body is violence against heaven itself.

Landscape

Im's cinematographer Chŏng Il-sŏng shoots the Korean countryside as a space where the sacred and political are inseparable. Mountains, fields, and rural villages are not backdrop. They are the terrain of the movement itself.

Key Terms

동학
Tonghak · "Eastern Learning"
Indigenous Korean religion founded by Ch'oe Che-u in 1860. Syncretized elements of Confucianism, Buddhism, Taoism, and Korean shamanism.
해월
Hae-wol · "Sea-Moon"
Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's religious name. The character at the center of the film.
천도교
Ch'ŏndogyo
"Religion of the Heavenly Way." What Donghak became under the third patriarch, Son Pyŏng-hŭi. Still active today.
포
P'o
Local Donghak cells or community units. The organizational structure that allowed the movement to survive underground for decades.

Discussion Questions

For class on Wednesday, February 11
History vs. Film
How does Im Kwon-taek's portrayal of Donghak compare to the primary sources we read for Day 14? What does the film emphasize that documents cannot? What do documents capture that the film cannot?
Think about emotion, scale, and the difference between reading a petition and watching a movement.
Theology as Politics
Donghak taught that every person contains the divine (innaech'ŏn). Why was this theological claim so politically threatening to the Chosŏn court? How does the film dramatize the connection between religious belief and political danger?
Consider how Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's followers are portrayed. Are they rebels? Believers? Both?
Women and Revolution
The film gives significant screen time to Ch'oe Si-hyŏng's three wives. What does their story tell us about the cost of revolutionary commitment? How does this relate to Donghak's promise of equality?
Does the movement practice what it preaches when it comes to gender?
The Filmmaker's Korea
Im Kwon-taek made this film in 1991, just four years after South Korea's democratic transition. Why might a filmmaker choose to tell the story of a 19th-century peasant uprising at that particular moment?
Think about 1987 (democratization), 1980 (Kwangju), and the long history of Korean popular movements.
Gaebyeok: Then and Now
The title 개벽 means "Great Opening," a new cosmic era. Where else in our course have we seen movements that promised a total transformation of society? How does Donghak's vision compare to, say, the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom?
Cross-regional comparison: millenarian movements in China, Korea, Japan.
Cinema and National Identity
Film critic David James called Im Kwon-taek "Korean National Cinema" itself. What does it mean for a single filmmaker to carry that weight? How does this film argue for what Korean identity is?
Consider Im's other films: Mandala (Buddhism), Sŏp'yŏnje (pansori), Ch'wihwasŏn (painting).
