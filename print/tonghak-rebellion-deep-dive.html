The Spark: A Corrupt Magistrate

In January 1894, the magistrate of Gobu county, Jo Byeong-gap, forced peasants to build a reservoir with their own labor‚Äîthen taxed them for using the water. When farmers petitioned against this blatant extortion, Jo had them beaten. On January 10, a thousand peasants stormed the county office, freed prisoners, distributed hoarded grain, and demolished the hated reservoir.

It was a local protest against a local injustice. Within six months, it would trigger a war between the two largest powers in Asia, kill tens of thousands, and begin the end of Korean independence.

"We do not rise to overthrow the king. We rise to punish corrupt officials who suck the blood of the people, and to drive out the foreign barbarians who violate our land." ‚Äî Jeon Bong-jun, Proclamation of the Peasant Army, March 1894

What Was Tonghak?

Tonghak (Êù±Â≠∏, "Eastern Learning") was Korea's answer to Western encroachment‚Äîa syncretic religion founded in 1860 by Ch'oe Cheu, a fallen yangban who experienced a mystical revelation. It combined elements of Confucianism, Buddhism, Daoism, and shamanistic traditions into something new: a populist faith teaching that "in nae ch'on"‚Äîhuman beings are heaven.

This was revolutionary. In a society where one's birth determined one's entire life, Tonghak declared that every person, from the lowest slave to the highest aristocrat, carried heaven within them. The social hierarchy that had governed Korea for centuries was, in Tonghak theology, a human corruption of divine truth.

Ch'oe Cheu was executed in 1864 for spreading heterodoxy. His successor, Ch'oe Sihyong, took the movement underground, where it spread quietly through Korea's southern provinces‚Äîthe agricultural heartland, and the region most exploited by corrupt officials.

From Protest to Revolution

What began in Gobu exploded outward. By March, Jeon Bong-jun‚Äîa village teacher so short his soldiers called him "Green Bean"‚Äîhad organized tens of thousands of peasants into a disciplined army. Armed mostly with bamboo spears and farming tools, they defeated government forces at Hwangnyongchon on April 7. On April 27, they captured Jeonju, the provincial capital.

Peasants had taken a major city. Jeon set up headquarters in the governor's office and began implementing reforms: freeing slaves, burning debt records, redistributing grain to the poor. For a brief moment, a different Korea seemed possible.

Then came the truce. On June 11, the government signed the Jeonju Peace Agreement. The rebels would disperse; in exchange, peasant self-governance bureaus (jibgang-so) would be established throughout Jeolla Province. Reform would come peacefully.

It might have worked. But by the time the ink was dry, foreign armies were already on Korean soil.

The War No One Wanted‚ÄîExcept Japan

By June 1894, the rebellion was over. The Jeonju Peace Agreement had worked. Jeon Bong-jun had disbanded the peasant army. The crisis that brought foreign troops was resolved.

But Japan refused to withdraw. On July 23, Japanese forces seized the Korean royal palace and installed a puppet government. On August 1, Japan declared war on China‚Äînot to suppress rebels, but to end Chinese influence in Korea forever.

The First Sino-Japanese War was a catastrophe for China. The "sick man of Asia" was humiliated by an upstart neighbor they had considered culturally inferior. In nine months, Japan destroyed the Qing navy, occupied Manchuria, and forced China to sign the Treaty of Shimonoseki (April 1895), recognizing Korean "independence" and ceding Taiwan.

"I wanted to save the people from misery, to punish the corrupt officials who squeeze them like oil from sesame, and to expel the foreign barbarians who treat our land like their playground. If this is treason, then I am proud to be a traitor." ‚Äî Jeon Bong-jun, at his trial, February 1895

The Second Rising‚Äîand the End

In October 1894, seeing Japanese troops occupying Korea, Jeon Bong-jun remobilized the peasant army. This time they fought not for reform, but for national survival.

It was hopeless. At the Battle of Gongju in November, peasants armed with bamboo spears faced Japanese troops with modern rifles and artillery. The slaughter was total‚Äîperhaps 30,000 dead. Jeon escaped, was betrayed by an informer, captured on December 28, and executed in Seoul on March 29, 1895.

Legacy: Different Koreas, Same Hero

South Korea

Jeon Bong-jun is celebrated as a proto-democratic hero. The 1980s democracy movement adopted him as a symbol. Statues stand in Jeonju and his hometown. The mung bean pancake (nokdujeon) is named for "General Green Bean."

North Korea

The DPRK honors Tonghak as a peasant class struggle against feudalism and imperialism‚Äîa precursor to the socialist revolution. The rebellion proves the revolutionary spirit of the Korean masses.

Cheondogyo

Tonghak survives today as Cheondogyo (Religion of the Heavenly Way), renamed in 1905. It was central to the March First Movement of 1919 and remains active in both Koreas.

The Big Question

Did the Tonghak rebels doom their cause by triggering foreign intervention? Or did foreign powers doom Korea regardless? The Jeonju Peace suggests reform was possible‚Äîuntil the whales started fighting.
