Penetrating Questions Key Points
1 Isa believes: "It is foolish for Japanese to speak this language like foreigners. If they do, it makes them foreigners, too." Why does he refuse to speak English? His refusal is principled resistance, not incompetence. Speaking English = loss of identity. Yet his resistance is self-defeating (English teacher who won't teach). Contrast Yamada's grotesque mimicry.
2 Was the occupation democratization or colonization - or both? The constitution granted rights; "The American School" shows teachers who felt "we lost." Both/and, not either/or. Americans imposed genuinely democratic reforms. Constitution American-drafted but Japanese-ratified. Yet psychological humiliation was real: walking 4 miles on roads built for American cars.
3 Yamada boasts of beheading "about twenty" POWs while seeking American approval. What does his character reveal? Accommodation without transformation. War criminal rebranded as English teacher. He wears leather shoes while colleagues wear army boots. Represents Japan's incomplete reckoning with the past.
Connections
From Plate 21: While China underwent Maoist revolution, Japan underwent American-supervised transformation. Two paths from mid-century crisis.
Broader theme: Can democracy be imposed? Is transformation without humiliation possible? How do defeated nations reconstruct identity?
To Plate 23: Mao's China - revolutionary path vs. Japan's imposed democratization. Different costs, different outcomes.
Teaching Notes
• Constitution is short - have students identify surprising provisions
• "The American School" is literary gold - read Isa's interior monologue aloud
• Ask: "Have you felt you had to perform a different identity in another language?"
• Yamada's casual POW beheading - connects to unresolved war responsibility
• 4-mile march: Japanese pedestrians on roads built for American cars
