Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 How was Tonghak similar to and different from the Taiping movement in China? Similarities: Both peasant-based, religious ideology justifying rebellion, social reform demands, millenarian elements, charismatic founders with divine revelations. Differences: Tonghak was explicitly 'Eastern Learning' vs. Taiping's Christianity-derived ideology; Tonghak smaller scale but triggered international war; Taiping nearly overthrew dynasty while Tonghak never threatened Seoul; Tonghak survived as religion (Cheondogyo) while Taiping completely destroyed.
2 Was Tonghak primarily anti-foreign, reformist, or revolutionary? All three elements present in tension. Anti-foreign: Founded to counter Western Learning, rebels demanded expulsion of Japanese/Western barbarians. Reformist: Jeonju Peace Agreement shows willingness to work within system for reform; demands focused on corrupt officials not overthrowing monarchy. Revolutionary: 'Human beings are heaven' challenged entire yangban social hierarchy; peasant self-governance bureaus were proto-democratic. The foreign intervention prevented us from seeing which tendency would have dominated.
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 13: Plate 13 examined China's Taiping Rebellion (1850-1864) - a massive religious-peasant uprising that nearly overthrew the Qing dynasty. The Tonghak Rebellion invites direct comparison: both were led by charismatic founders who claimed divine revelation, both mobilized peasants with promises of social reform, both faced brutal suppression. Key differences: Taiping was influenced by Christianity while Tonghak explicitly rejected Western Learning; Taiping was larger and came closer to success; but Tonghak's international consequences were more dramatic. The suppression of Taiping relied on Western-trained forces and created regional strongmen; Tonghak suppression required foreign intervention that triggered war. Ask students: Why did a smaller rebellion in Korea have larger international consequences than the massive Taiping uprising?
Context: The Tonghak Rebellion exemplifies several course themes: (1) How domestic crisis invites foreign intervention that transforms local conflicts into international wars; (2) The emergence of indigenous responses to Western imperialism that draw on traditional resources while creating something new; (3) The tension between reform and revolution - Tonghak leaders signed the Jeonju Peace Agreement hoping for reform, but foreign intervention foreclosed that possibility; (4) Korea's tragic position as battleground for larger powers - 'shrimp among whales'; (5) How historical memory serves present politics - both Koreas claim Tonghak for radically different purposes.
To Plate 15: Plate 15 (Joseon's Slow Evolution) examines Korea's attempts at reform in the aftermath of the Sino-Japanese War. The Gabo Reforms (1894-1896) implemented many changes the Tonghak rebels had demanded - abolition of slavery, end of status discrimination, modernization of government - but under Japanese pressure rather than Korean initiative. Students should consider: Did Tonghak 'fail' if many of its reform demands were eventually enacted? What's the difference between reform achieved through rebellion vs. reform imposed by foreign power?
Notes
• The film 'Run High, Run Far' (Plate 16) depicts the Tonghak Rebellion - preview it here so students know to watch for key themes
• Ch'oe Cheu's revelation narrative parallels Hong Xiuquan's - both sick, received divine message, founded new religion. But Ch'oe explicitly rejected Christianity; Hong adapted it. Ask students why this difference matters.
• The doctrine 'in nae ch'on' (human beings are heaven) is revolutionary - it challenges the entire yangban social order. Connect to modern human rights discourse.
• Jeon Bong-jun's nickname 'General Green Bean' (Nokdu Janggun) humanizes him - he was short but commanded loyalty. South Korean democracy activists adopted him as symbol.
• The Jeonju Peace Agreement shows rebels weren't trying to overthrow the state - they wanted reform. Foreign intervention, not rebel intransigence, prevented peaceful resolution.
