Penetrating Questions Very short answers
1 Why did Japan avoid a war like the Opium War? Japan observed China's fate and learned. Geographic factors (island nation) mattered. Internal debates produced consensus for negotiation. But also: US was less aggressive than Britain, more interested in coaling stations than opium.
2 What did the daimyo responses reveal about divisions within Japan? Some wanted resistance ('expel the barbarians'), others accommodation, others reform. These debates would fuel the Meiji Restoration. Note: Many who initially opposed treaties later led modernization.
Causes / Effects / Connections
From Plate 10: Plate 10 showed China's traumatic 'opening' via the Opium War. Japan faced similar pressure but responded very differently. Why?
Context: Japan and China faced the same challenge (Western pressure) with different outcomes. Understanding why requires examining internal structures, leadership, and choices—not cultural destiny.
To Plate 12: Plate 12 examines Meiji Transformation—the reforms that made Japan the first non-Western industrial power. The Black Ships set this in motion.
Notes
• Show images of Black Ships—the visual contrast between steam warships and Japanese vessels is striking.
• The daimyo responses show real debate, not monolithic 'Japan.' Have students identify different positions.
• Be careful with 'Japan succeeded, China failed' framing—both faced genuine dilemmas with no easy answers.
• MIT Visualizing Cultures 'Black Ships and Samurai' has excellent images and analysis.
• Connect to contemporary Japan-US relations: the alliance began with gunboat diplomacy.
