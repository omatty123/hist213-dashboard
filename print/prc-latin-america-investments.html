5 Investments That Tell the Story

How China built a strategic position across Latin America

Ports, mines, power grids, solar farms, lithium. Five projects, three countries, one pattern.

The Scale

China-Latin America trade hit a record $518 billion in 2024. But the raw number obscures the strategy. Chinese investment isn't scattered. It targets chokepoints: the minerals that power the energy transition, the ports that move them, and the electricity grids that connect everything.

The Five Projects

1 — PORT

Port of Chancay

Chancay, Lima Region, Peru

The largest deep-water port on South America's Pacific coast. Inaugurated November 2024 with Xi Jinping and Peruvian President Boluarte presiding. Cuts shipping time to China by up to 20 days. Already being called "the Singapore of Latin America."

Investment
$3.5 billion
Chinese Company
COSCO Shipping (60%)
Capacity
1M TEUs (Phase 1), 3.5M at full buildout
Status
Operational (2024)
Why it matters: China now controls the Pacific gateway for South American exports. Paired with Las Bambas (below), Beijing owns both the extraction and the export logistics in Peru. The U.S. has formally warned Peru about Chinese operational control with limited oversight.

Sources: AidData, People's Map of Global China

2 — COPPER MINE

Las Bambas Copper Mine

Cotabambas, Apurimac Region, Peru (4,000m elevation)

One of the world's largest copper mines. Acquired from Glencore Xstrata in 2014 for $5.85 billion, financed by a $7 billion syndicated loan from four Chinese state banks. At the time, the largest overseas mining acquisition by any Chinese entity.

Acquisition Price
$5.85 billion
Chinese Company
MMG / China Minmetals (62.5%)
Output
300,000+ tonnes/yr (2% of global copper)
Status
Operational (since 2016)
Why it matters: Copper is essential for EVs, renewable energy, and electronics. A single mine gives China 2% of the world's copper supply and 15% of Peru's entire copper output. This contributes roughly 1% of Peru's GDP, giving Beijing deep economic leverage in Peruvian politics.

Sources: People's Map of Global China, FinanceAsia

3 — POWER GRID

Belo Monte Ultra-High Voltage Transmission

Pará State to Rio de Janeiro/São Paulo, Brazil (2,500+ km)

State Grid Corporation of China built and operates Brazil's UHV transmission backbone: two lines spanning over 4,600 km combined, carrying hydroelectric power from the Amazon to the industrial southeast. State Grid has invested over $12.5 billion in Brazil and controls 10% of the national high-voltage grid.

Investment
$12.5B+ (State Grid total in Brazil)
Chinese Company
State Grid Corporation of China
Capacity
4 GW per phase, 160+ TWh delivered
Status
Operational (2017/2019), expanding
Why it matters: This is not a single project. It is a structural position inside the electricity backbone of Latin America's largest economy. The UHV technology is exclusively Chinese (no other country deploys ±800 kV DC at this scale), creating long-term technological dependency. Brazil's clean energy transition literally runs on Chinese-built infrastructure.

Sources: NS Energy, SASAC, Dialogue Earth

4 — SOLAR FARM

Cauchari Solar Park

Puna Plateau, Jujuy Province, Argentina (4,020m elevation)

Latin America's largest solar park, and the world's highest-altitude large-scale solar installation. Over 1.18 million photovoltaic panels across 600 hectares. Financed primarily by the Export-Import Bank of China ($331.5 million loan), built by PowerChina and Shanghai Electric, with Huawei control systems.

Investment
~$540 million
Chinese Companies
PowerChina, Shanghai Electric, Huawei
Capacity
300 MW (expanding to 500 MW)
Status
Operational (2020), expanding
Why it matters: The Exim Bank financing model here is the template for Chinese clean energy investment across the Global South. China positions itself as the builder of Latin America's energy transition, not just its resource extractor. The Jujuy location sits next to Argentina's lithium triangle, connecting China's solar and lithium interests in a single region.

Sources: NS Energy, PowerChina, CleanTechnica

5 — LITHIUM MINE

Cauchari-Olaroz Lithium Operation

Jujuy Province, Argentina (the "Lithium Triangle")

China's largest lithium producer, Ganfeng Lithium, controls this brine extraction operation producing battery-grade lithium carbonate. 80% of production goes directly to China. In Q4 2025, the mine hit 97% of nameplate capacity. Ganfeng is consolidating additional projects in the region, targeting 150,000 tonnes/year.

Investment
$1.8B+ across the basin
Chinese Company
Ganfeng Lithium (controlling partner)
Output
34,100 tonnes/yr, tripling to 85K by 2029
Status
Operational, rapidly expanding
Why it matters: Lithium is the critical mineral for EV batteries and energy storage. China already dominates lithium processing globally; controlling extraction at the source closes the entire supply chain. With 80% of output feeding Chinese battery manufacturers (CATL, BYD), this is not a market operation. It is a dedicated pipeline. Combined with CATL's $1.4 billion deal for the Salar del Hombre Muerto deposit (signed February 2025), China is assembling dominant control over Argentina's lithium reserves.

Sources: Mining.com, ISDP, Ganfeng Lithium

Context: Why the "New Imperialism" Frame Fails

Western commentary routinely compares Chinese investment in Latin America to 19th-century British imperialism. The comparison is structurally lazy. British "investment" in Latin America arrived with gunboats, debt traps enforced by military blockade (Venezuela 1902), and infrastructure designed exclusively for extraction. Railways ran from mines to ports and nowhere else. Local governments had no say in the terms.

These five projects involve bilateral agreements between sovereign governments. Peru auctioned the Chancay port concession. Brazil auctioned its transmission contracts. Argentina sought Exim Bank financing for Cauchari. The host countries set the regulatory terms. China also builds infrastructure these countries actually need for their own development: UHV grids, solar capacity, port modernization. That is not what the British East India Company was doing.

This does not mean the relationships are equal or uncomplicated. Concentration of critical supply chains in one partner's hands creates dependency regardless of intent. When 80% of a lithium mine's output flows to a single country, the host government's bargaining position narrows over time. These are real questions of economic sovereignty, and Latin American governments debate them vigorously.

But "is this imperialism?" is the wrong question. It flattens the difference between coercion and commerce, and it conveniently frames any non-Western economic power as inherently predatory. The better questions are about terms, agency, and alternatives.

Student questions: Who sets the terms of these deals, and can host governments renegotiate? What alternatives do Peru, Brazil, and Argentina have for the capital and technology these projects require? What does it tell you that the "imperialism" label gets applied to Chinese port construction but not to, say, U.S. military bases across Latin America?
