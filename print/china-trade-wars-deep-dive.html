Active Trade Wars & Disputes

1. US-China Trade War (2018–Present)

The Big One

Escalating

The largest trade conflict in modern history, affecting over $600 billion in bilateral trade.

July 2018

US imposes 25% tariffs on $34B of Chinese goods. China retaliates with equivalent tariffs. Trade war begins.

September 2018

US levies 10% tariffs on $200B more Chinese imports (later raised to 25%). China retaliates on $60B of US goods.

Late 2019

Peak escalation: US tariffs of 15-30% on $550B; China tariffs of 5-25% on $185B of US goods.

January 2020

"Phase One" deal signed. US reduces some tariffs; China commits to buy more US goods (commitments largely unmet).

2021–2024

Biden administration maintains Trump tariffs. Average US tariff on China: ~19%. Targeted increases on EVs, solar, batteries in 2024.

February 2025

Trump 2.0: 10% tariff citing fentanyl emergency. Raised to 20% in March.

April 2025

Peak: US tariffs hit 145%; China retaliates at 125%. China suspends critical mineral exports.

May 2025

Geneva negotiations: 90-day reduction to 30% (US) and 10% (China). Extended to November 2025.

Current (Jan 2026)

US tariffs: 47.5% on all Chinese goods. China tariffs: 31.9% on all US goods.

Sources: Peterson Institute, Wikipedia, Tax Foundation

By the Numbers: US-China Tariff Escalation

Before trade war (2017): Average US tariff on China: ~3%

2. EU-China EV Dispute (2023–Present)

Electric Vehicle Tariffs

WTO Panel Active

Background: Chinese EV market share in EU surged from 3.5% (2020) to 27.2% (Q2 2024). EU launched anti-subsidy investigation in October 2023.

EU Action (October 2024): Countervailing duties for 5 years:

  • BYD: 17.4%
  • Geely: 19.9%
  • SAIC: 37.6%
  • Other cooperating producers: 20.8%
  • Non-cooperating: 37.6%

China Retaliation: Anti-dumping probes into EU pork, dairy, and brandy (targeting French Cognac with temporary duties).

Current Status: WTO panel established April 2025; composed October 2025. 17 countries reserved third-party rights.

Sources: WTO DS630, CSIS

3. Canada-China Canola & EV Dispute (2024–Present)

Tit-for-Tat Escalation

Escalating

Canada's Move: 100% tariff on Chinese EVs; 25% on steel and aluminum.

China's Retaliation:

  • 100% duties on Canadian canola oil, canola meal, and peas
  • 25% tariffs on pork and seafood
  • Anti-dumping investigation into canola seed (September 2024)

Impact: China buys 65% of Canada's canola seed exports ($15.8B total canola exports in 2023). Exports to China collapsed 63% between 2024-2025.

Source: Canola Council of Canada, MLT Aikins

4. Australia-China Trade War (2020–2024) — RESOLVED

Barley, Wine, and COVID Origins

Resolved

Trigger: Australia called for international COVID-19 origin investigation (April 2020). China's ambassador suggested boycott.

China's Actions:

  • 80.5% tariff on Australian barley (May 2020)
  • 206% tariff on Australian wine (November 2020)
  • Informal restrictions on coal, beef, lobster, timber, copper

Resolution:

  • Barley tariffs removed: August 2023
  • Wine tariffs removed: March 2024

Outcome: Australian exporters diversified to new markets during restrictions. When China reopened, 76% of barley exports returned to China due to price premiums.

Sources: Australian Foreign Minister, Wikipedia

The WTO's Paralysis

The US has blocked appointments to the WTO Appellate Body since 2019, effectively paralyzing the dispute resolution system. Cases can be won at the panel level but "appealed into the void" with no resolution. Both the US and China now operate with little risk of binding legal consequence.

Historical Echo: From Tea to Semiconductors

In the 18th century, Britain had a trade deficit problem with China. China wanted silver; Britain wanted tea, silk, and porcelain. The "solution" was opium—a product China didn't want but couldn't stop.

Today, the tables have turned in some ways: The US runs a $295 billion deficit with China (2024). China dominates critical minerals and manufacturing. The US uses tariffs; China uses export controls.

The question for students: What are the continuities and discontinuities between Qing-era trade conflicts and today's trade wars? Who has leverage? How do states respond when trade becomes a weapon?
